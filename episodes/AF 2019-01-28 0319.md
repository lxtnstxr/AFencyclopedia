## Summary

Nicholas J. Fuentes attended a secret meetup in Chicago with Patrick Casey and Jared Taylor. A FiveThirtyEight analysis by Nate Silver found public blame on Donald Trump for a 35-day government shutdown increased to over 50%. The Trump administration announced sanctions on Venezuela's PDVSA following U.S. recognition of Juan Guaidó as interim president.

---

(00:00:06) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] began his broadcast titled "President Jared Kushner," outlining a show focused on grievances with the [[Donald Trump|Trump]] administration's foreign policy and immigration actions.

(00:30:38) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] attended a secret weekend meetup in downtown [[Chicago]] with [[Patrick Casey]] of Identity Europa and [[Jared Taylor]] of American Renaissance, where he participated in a speaking engagement.

(00:33:14) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] criticized a tweet by [[Mike Cernovich]] that attacked [[Christianity]] and suggested a moderated form of [[Islam]] was the West's only hope, calling it an example of a double standard where insulting Christianity is acceptable but criticizing other religions is not.

(00:35:14) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that figures like [[Bill Maher]], [[Family Guy]], and Hollywood celebrities cowardly use comedy to push around Christians, a tactic he described as neither funny nor edgy.

(00:35:56) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated that the 35-day federal government shutdown ended on a Friday, a decision that followed a pattern where [[Donald Trump]] takes an action that causes initial panic, which [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] then attempts to explain.

(00:36:38) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] reported that [[Sarah Huckabee Sanders]] stated if [[Congress]] did not reach a border funding deal by February 15th, [[Donald Trump]] would declare a national emergency to build the wall.

(00:37:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] cited a [[FiveThirtyEight]] analysis by [[Nate Silver]] which tracked polling during the shutdown and found that public blame on [[Donald Trump]] for the shutdown increased from roughly 48-49% at the start to over 50% by its end.

(00:38:20) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that the government shutdown strategy failed because public discontent, which was supposed to pressure [[Democratic Party|Democrats]], backfired and turned against [[Donald Trump]], eliminating his leverage.

(00:39:40) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] reported that [[McClatchy]] and [[Breitbart]] published stories alleging [[Jared Kushner]] revived a rejected proposal to offer permanent protection to 1.8 million "Dreamers" in exchange for $25 billion in border security, including some wall funding.

(00:40:18) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated that [[Lindsey Graham]], a co-sponsor of the Gang of Eight amnesty bill, was reportedly pushing the same immigration deal as [[Jared Kushner]] behind closed doors while publicly advocating for a state of emergency to fund the wall.

(00:41:22) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] described the optics as terrible after [[Donald Trump]] reopened the government and [[Nancy Pelosi]] subsequently stated she had only agreed to find a "mutually agreeable date" for the State of the Union, not to schedule it immediately.

(00:42:16) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] reported that [[Nancy Pelosi]] invited [[Donald Trump]] to deliver the State of the Union address on February 5th, which he accepted.

(00:43:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that pressure from commentators like [[Ann Coulter]] and others who have the ear of [[Donald Trump]] is useful and effective, contrasting it with calls to abandon electoral politics.

(00:44:20) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated that while achievements like tax cuts and moving the [[U.S. Embassy]] to [[Jerusalem]] could have been done by a generic [[Republican Party|Republican]] president like [[Ted Cruz]] or [[Marco Rubio]], the core "America First" promises on issues like immigration remained unfulfilled.

(00:45:46) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] reported that the [[Donald Trump|Trump]] administration announced devastating sanctions on [[Venezuela]]'s state-owned oil company PDVSA, aimed at pressuring [[Nicolás Maduro]] to step down, following the U.S. recognition of opposition leader [[Juan Guaidó]] as interim president.

(00:46:20) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] expressed support for diplomatic recognition and economic sanctions in [[Venezuela]] as proportional measures that serve [[United States|U.S.]] interests in the Western Hemisphere, but drew a line at military intervention.

(00:47:12) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] cited a statement by [[John Bolton]] that "all options are on the table" regarding [[Venezuela]] and noted that Bolton was photographed with a notepad reading "5,000 troops to Colombia," which [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] viewed as a step towards unjustified military involvement.

(00:48:36) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that the [[United States|U.S.]] military presence across the globe—in [[Syria]], [[Iraq]], [[Afghanistan]], [[Egypt]], [[Somalia]], [[Libya]], [[Niger]], and [[Yemen]]—is unsustainable and diverts focus from domestic issues like the border crisis.

(00:49:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] reported that, despite [[Donald Trump]]'s December pledge to withdraw all [[United States|U.S.]] forces from [[Syria]], the administration was considering a plan to keep 200-300 troops at the Al-Tanf garrison to counter [[Iran]]ian activity.

(00:50:20) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] framed the political struggle as a multi-generational war against a deeply entrenched establishment, stating that the [[MAGA]] movement lacks the infrastructure, donor networks, and think tanks of its opponents and must prepare for a long conflict.

(00:51:12) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] mentioned plans to discuss the 2020 election, [[Howard Schultz]], and [[Hillary Clinton]], but stated he would save those topics for a future show.
