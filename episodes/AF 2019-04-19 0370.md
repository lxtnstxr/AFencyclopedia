## Summary

Jordan B. Peterson debated Slavoj Žižek in an event moderated by Stephen Blackwood. Žižek holds doctorates from the University of Ljubljana and the University of Paris. Nicholas J. Fuentes argued that contemporary populism and migration were defining political issues, not the economic ideologies of Peterson and Žižek.

(00:54:31) [[Jordan B. Peterson]] debated [[Slavoj Žižek]] in a pay-per-view event moderated by [[Stephen Blackwood]] on the topics of happiness, capitalism, and communism.

(00:54:31) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] paid $15 to watch the [[Jordan B. Peterson]] versus [[Slavoj Žižek]] debate but was forced to watch an illicit YouTube stream after the official website crashed.

(01:11:85) The [[Jordan B. Peterson]] versus [[Slavoj Žižek]] debate featured 30-minute opening statements from each participant, followed by 10-minute rebuttals and a disorganized cross-talk session.

(01:21:25) [[Jordan B. Peterson]] argued that postmodern neo-Marxists in academia had replaced classical Marxists by swapping economic class struggle for a cultural framework of oppressor versus oppressed.

(01:47:93) [[Slavoj Žižek]] holds a doctorate in philosophy from the University of Ljubljana and a doctorate in psychoanalysis from the University of Paris.

(02:27:47) [[Jordan B. Peterson]] prepared for his debate with [[Slavoj Žižek]] by reading and critiquing the ten presuppositions in the Communist Manifesto by [[Karl Marx]] and [[Friedrich Engels]].

(03:03:79) [[Jordan B. Peterson]] defended capitalism by citing United Nations Millennium Development Goals showing a reduction in global poverty.

(03:21:51) [[Slavoj Žižek]] argued that the fundamental premise of the debate was flawed by attacking the concept of happiness itself, suggesting that getting what one desires may not lead to satisfaction.

(03:28:49) [[Slavoj Žižek]] posited that the burden of modernity is freedom itself and that people's real struggle is to define their duty and obligation in the absence of authoritarian direction.

(04:24:97) [[Slavoj Žižek]] acknowledged problems caused by immigrants but argued the real underlying issues are created by capitalism, which projects societal dissonance onto migrant populations.

(04:26:35) [[Slavoj Žižek]] characterized the narrative of cultural Marxism originating from the Frankfurt School as a myth and a scapegoat for America's moral degeneration.

(04:28:35) [[Slavoj Žižek]] argued that America's moral decline has more to do with the disruptive forces of capitalism and technological progress than with the influence of Jewish intellectuals from the Frankfurt School.

(04:29:47) [[Slavoj Žižek]] described [[Donald Trump]] as the ultimate postmodern president for promoting a narrative of traditional values while embodying personal vulgarity and contradiction.

(04:29:55) [[Slavoj Žižek]] pointed to global issues in places like the [[Congo]] and [[Yemen]] as examples of capitalism outsourcing misery and creating a dystopian world order.

(04:30:47) [[Slavoj Žižek]] concluded that he is not a communist but advocates for a regulated market and increased government oversight to address global existential challenges like ecological disaster and corporate power.

(05:32:17) During his rebuttal, [[Jordan B. Peterson]] argued that capitalism is not a perfect system but is the best one discovered so far, comparing it to [[Winston Churchill]]'s defense of democracy.

(05:34:31) [[Slavoj Žižek]] used the example of late Cold War [[Czechoslovakia]] to argue that relative prosperity and freedom, combined with a reasonable amount of strife against the government, could produce a form of happiness.

(05:36:59) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] observed that [[Jordan B. Peterson]] and [[Slavoj Žižek]] fundamentally agreed on secularism, modernism, equality, multiculturalism, and democracy, with their only disagreement being the degree of government intervention in the economy.

(05:42:51) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that the election of [[Donald Trump]], the rise of populism in [[Europe]], [[India]], and [[Latin America]], and the post-2015 migrant crisis were the defining political issues of the era, not the economic ideologies debated by [[Jordan B. Peterson]] and [[Slavoj Žižek]].
