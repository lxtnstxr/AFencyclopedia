## Summary

The United States intelligence community plans to purchase data from private technology companies to circumvent legal restrictions on domestic surveillance. The Department of Homeland Security is exploring the use of outside firms to monitor online political narratives and extremist chatter, a practice critics argue targets specific domestic political views. Nicholas J. Fuentes characterized the U.S. government and allied institutions as a unified regime engaged in political persecution and narrative control.

---

(00:01:40) [[CNN]] reported that the [[United States]] intelligence community, including the [[Federal Bureau of Investigation|FBI]] and the [[National Security Agency|NSA]], plans to circumvent legal restrictions on domestic surveillance by purchasing data from private technology companies like [[Google]] and [[Facebook]].

(00:01:40) The [[Department of Homeland Security|DHS]], [[Federal Bureau of Investigation|FBI]], and [[National Security Agency|NSA]] are utilizing private companies to spy on American citizens, a practice that would be illegal if conducted directly by the agencies themselves.

(00:42:20) The [[Joe Biden|Biden administration]] is considering using outside firms to track online "extremist chatter" by Americans, a move described by [[CNN]] as expanding government intelligence gathering while potentially drawing criticism over domestic surveillance.

(00:44:20) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that the [[United States]] government defines "extremist chatter" to include the political views of [[Donald Trump|Trump]] supporters, QAnon adherents, and individuals who believe the 2020 election was fraudulent.

(00:46:40) The [[Department of Homeland Security|DHS]] is limited by law in monitoring citizens online without justification and is banned from infiltrating private messaging apps used by groups like the [[Proud Boys]]; a proposed plan would allow the department to circumvent these limits by using outside entities.

(00:48:40) The [[Department of Homeland Security|DHS]] aims to monitor emerging political narratives online, a function [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] characterized as moving the agency into the business of political narrative control rather than solely preventing terrorism.

(00:50:40) [[Tom Warrick]], a senior fellow at the [[Atlantic Council]] and former [[Department of Homeland Security|DHS]] Deputy Assistant Secretary for Counterterrorism, stated there was limited awareness before January 6th of what violent extremists were planning on social media and that [[Department of Homeland Security|DHS]] should explore whether contractors could help understand plots and trends.

(00:51:40) [[Jared Holt]], an activist who previously wrote for [[Right Wing Watch]], works for the [[Atlantic Council]], an organization that [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated is funded by corporations like [[J.P. Morgan]] and [[Pfizer]], as well as [[NATO]] and the [[United States Department of Defense|Department of Defense]].

(00:53:40) The [[Department of Homeland Security|DHS]] is working with non-profit research firms that create false identities to infiltrate private social media groups on platforms like [[Telegram]], screenshot content, and provide the information to intelligence agencies.

(00:54:40) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] described an "iron triangle" where elite universities like [[MIT]] and [[Harvard]] develop AI algorithms for digital forensics, social media companies like [[Facebook]] implement them, and federal intelligence agencies contract and use the resulting data to identify and target political dissent.

(01:04:40) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that the [[United States]] regime, comprising the intelligence apparatus, major corporations, elite universities, and think tanks like the [[Atlantic Council]], constitutes a unified power structure with the objective of total political persecution and narrative control.

(01:15:40) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated that American dissidents are not safe anywhere under [[United States]] jurisdiction or within the reach of the [[Central Intelligence Agency|CIA]], and that the only countries offering potential safety are [[Russia]], [[China]], [[Iran]], and [[North Korea]].

(01:16:40) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that for political change to occur in the [[United States]], the global power of the American regime must wane, potentially through the expanded influence of [[Russia]] and [[China]] or a nationalist movement in [[Europe]].

(01:18:40) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] characterized the [[United States]] as an "evil empire" analogous to the Soviet Union, citing the hoisting of BLM and LGBTQ flags below the American flag at [[United States]] embassies as symbolic of the regime's ideology.
