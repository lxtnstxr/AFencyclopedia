## Summary

Nancy Pelosi announced a Democratic House impeachment inquiry into a phone call between Donald Trump and Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy regarding Joe Biden's son, Hunter Biden. Donald Trump released a transcript of the call and the whistleblower complaint, a document Democrats argued was incomplete despite former CIA officers stating the White House system ensured its accuracy. Joe Biden, as Vice President, pressured Ukraine to fire a prosecutor investigating Burisma Holdings, where Hunter Biden was a board member paid up to $50,000 monthly. The Trump campaign raised $5 million in donations within 24 hours of Pelosi's impeachment announcement.

(00:19:22) [[Nancy Pelosi]] announced that the [[Democratic Party|Democrats]] in the [[United States House of Representatives|House]] were formally launching an impeachment inquiry into a phone call between [[Donald Trump|President Trump]] and the Ukrainian president regarding [[Joe Biden]]'s son, [[Hunter Biden]].

(00:20:28) [[Donald Trump|President Trump]] released a transcript of his July 25th phone call with Ukrainian President [[Volodymyr Zelenskyy|Zelenskyy]], as well as the whistleblower complaint that had initially alleged a quid pro quo regarding [[Hunter Biden]] and U.S. aid to [[Ukraine]].

(00:32:48) The transcript of the call, described as a five-page memorandum, contained no explicit quid pro quo linking an investigation of [[Joe Biden]] and [[Hunter Biden]] to the release of roughly $400 million in U.S. aid to [[Ukraine]].

(00:33:58) Following the transcript's release, [[Democratic Party|Democrats]] argued the document was incomplete and questioned whether the [[White House]] was hiding evidence, despite two former [[Central Intelligence Agency|CIA]] officers stating the White House system ensured the transcript was likely verbatim.

(00:38:56) The anonymous whistleblower whose complaint sparked the impeachment inquiry was represented by a legal team that included Democratic operatives who had worked for [[Chuck Schumer]] and [[Hillary Clinton]].

(00:40:00) [[Andrew Bakaj]], a managing partner at Compass Rose Legal Group who interned for [[Chuck Schumer]] and [[Hillary Clinton]] in 2001, was part of the legal team representing the whistleblower.

(00:41:58) During the Obama administration, [[Joe Biden]], while Vice President, pressured the Ukrainian government to fire a prosecutor who had been investigating the gas company [[Burisma Holdings]], where his son [[Hunter Biden]] served on the board, or risk losing U.S. aid.

(00:49:18) In the 24 hours following [[Nancy Pelosi]]'s impeachment announcement, the [[Donald Trump|Trump]] campaign raised $5 million in donations.

(00:51:56) [[Hunter Biden]] joined the board of Ukrainian gas company [[Burisma Holdings]] in 2014 and was paid as much as $50,000 per month for his services.

(00:52:20) In 2008, during [[Barack Obama]]'s presidential campaign, officials addressed questions about [[Hunter Biden]] receiving a $100,000 annual retainer from credit card company [[MBNA]], while his father [[Joe Biden]] helped pass legislation that made it more difficult for individuals to declare bankruptcy.

(01:03:20) [[Carson King]], a 24-year-old from [[Iowa]], held up a sign with his Venmo address during an [[ESPN]] College GameDay broadcast, leading to donations he pledged to the University of Iowa Stead Family Children's Hospital.

(01:04:44) [[Anheuser-Busch]] and [[Venmo]] pledged to match the donations [[Carson King]] raised for the University of Iowa Stead Family Children's Hospital, potentially tripling the total funds.

(01:05:32) [[Aaron Calvin]], a reporter for the [[Des Moines Register]], discovered and publicized a controversial 2011 tweet by [[Carson King]] that referenced a racially charged segment from the television show *Tosh.0*.

(01:06:00) Following the revelation of his 2011 tweet, [[Carson King]] held a press conference, calling the tweet "hurtful and embarrassing" and apologizing, while [[Anheuser-Busch]] announced it would honor its donation commitment but cut all ties with him.

(01:07:00) After publicizing [[Carson King]]'s old tweets, reporter [[Aaron Calvin]] was himself found to have posted offensive tweets between 2010 and 2013, including using a racist slur, leading him to delete the posts and apologize.
