## Summary

The Born-Alive Abortion Survivors Protection Act failed in the United States Senate by a vote of 52 to 44. The Indian Air Force conducted airstrikes on Pakistan in retaliation for a suicide bombing in Kashmir that killed 40 Indian soldiers. Nicholas J. Fuentes was banned from attending the CPAC conference in Washington, D.C.

---

(00:05:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] announced the final show of the week, which would cover geopolitical tensions in [[India]], [[Pakistan]], and [[Kashmir]], as well as a failed "born alive" abortion bill in the [[United States]].

(00:29:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated he would be traveling to see [[CPAC]] the following morning and would be absent from the show until the following Monday.

(00:41:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] introduced the Born-Alive Abortion Survivors Protection Act, a bill that would have required healthcare practitioners to provide medical care to infants who survive abortion attempts.

(00:55:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] reported that the Born-Alive Abortion Survivors Protection Act failed in the [[United States Senate]] by a vote of 52 to 44, with nearly all [[Democratic Party|Democrats]] voting against it.

(00:58:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] identified [[Joe Manchin]], [[Bob Casey Jr.]], and [[Doug Jones]] as the three [[Democratic Party|Democratic]] senators who voted in favor of the Born-Alive Abortion Survivors Protection Act.

(01:04:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] noted that [[Lisa Murkowski]], [[Dan Sullivan]], and [[Tim Scott]] were the three [[Republican Party|Republican]] senators absent for the vote on the Born-Alive Abortion Survivors Protection Act.

(01:21:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] cited a [[Marist]] poll showing a shift in American public opinion, with the percentage identifying as "pro-life" rising from 38% to 47% in approximately one month, a change largely driven by [[Democratic Party|Democrats]] under the age of 45.

(01:24:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that the essential difference between his movement and the alt-right was his grounding in [[Christianity]] and opposition to abortion, which he stated some in the alt-right supported for demographic reasons.

(01:25:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] provided context for the conflict between [[India]] and [[Pakistan]], noting the countries have been in a state of hostility since independence, with a recent escalation triggered by a Valentine's Day suicide bombing in [[Kashmir]] that killed 40 Indian soldiers.

(01:28:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] reported that the [[Indian Air Force]] conducted airstrikes on [[Pakistan]] in retaliation for the [[Kashmir]] suicide bombing, marking the first such incursion into Pakistani territory in roughly 50 years.

(01:30:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] compared the [[India|Indian]] airstrikes, which [[Pakistan]] claimed caused no casualties, to a 2017 missile strike ordered by [[Donald Trump]] on [[Syria]] that he said also hit evacuated targets.

(01:38:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated that [[Pakistan]]'s Prime Minister [[Imran Khan]] promised a response to the [[India|Indian]] airstrikes and convened the [[National Command Authority]], the body overseeing [[Pakistan]]'s nuclear arsenal.

(01:50:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] connected the [[India]]-[[Pakistan]] conflict to ongoing U.S. peace talks with the [[Taliban]] in [[Afghanistan]], suggesting [[Pakistan]] had threatened to disrupt those talks if [[India]]'s retaliation was too severe.

(01:54:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that the measured nature of [[India]]'s airstrike was a positive sign that [[Pakistan]] would not interfere with, and might even facilitate, the U.S. negotiations with the [[Taliban]] for a withdrawal from [[Afghanistan]].

(01:59:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] contrasted the foreign policy of [[Donald Trump]], which he characterized as actively managing global crises toward peaceful outcomes, with that of [[Barack Obama]], which he said led to destabilizing events like [[Russia]]'s invasion of [[Ukraine]] and [[Iran]]'s expanded influence.

(02:03:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] revealed he had been banned from attending the [[CPAC]] conference, but would be holding a meetup for his followers at the associated hotel in [[Washington, D.C.]].

(02:07:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] speculated his ban from [[CPAC]] was likely due to a [[HuffPost]] article published after the previous year's event that listed him among "white nationalists" attending the conference.
