## Summary

A former boyfriend of Christine Blasey Ford submitted a letter to the Senate Judiciary Committee alleging her testimony about Brett Kavanaugh contained contradictions. Donald Trump publicly questioned Ford's testimony at a Mississippi rally, drawing criticism from Senators Lindsey Graham, Susan Collins, and Jeff Flake. Congressional staffer Jackson Cosko was charged with doxing senators after their personal information was posted online from a U.S. Capitol IP address. Nicholas J. Fuentes argued opposition to Brett Kavanaugh's Supreme Court confirmation was driven by concerns he would overturn *Roe v. Wade*.

---

(00:00:06) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] opened his show by stating that the confirmation of [[Brett Kavanaugh]] to the [[Supreme Court of the United States|Supreme Court]] was the dominant news story, with an [[Federal Bureau of Investigation|FBI]] investigation believed to be concluded and a report pending distribution to senators.

(00:49:18) A new letter sent to the [[United States Senate Committee on the Judiciary|Senate Judiciary Committee]] under penalty of perjury from a former boyfriend of [[Christine Blasey Ford]] alleged that she never mentioned a sexual assault by [[Brett Kavanaugh]] during their six-year relationship, coached a friend on how to pass a polygraph test, and had no fear of flying or enclosed spaces, contradicting her testimony.

(00:57:50) [[Donald Trump]] commented on [[Christine Blasey Ford]]'s testimony at a rally in [[Mississippi]], noting she remembered drinking one beer but could not recall key details of the alleged assault, remarks which drew criticism from figures like [[Lindsey Graham]], [[Susan Collins]], and [[Jeff Flake]].

(01:14:48) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] presented a diagram categorizing individuals in the [[Brett Kavanaugh]] confirmation process, listing [[Christine Blasey Ford]], [[Deborah Ramirez]], and [[Julie Swetnick]] as accusers to be believed unquestionably, while listing [[Brett Kavanaugh]], his friend [[Mark Judge]], and others who denied the allegations or supported Kavanaugh as not to be believed.

(01:21:24) Polling from [[NPR]] indicated that Republican enthusiasm for the midterm elections surged, with the percentage of [[Republican Party (United States)|Republicans]] saying the elections were "very important" jumping from 68% to 80%, erasing a prior Democratic advantage.

(01:28:07) During the [[Brett Kavanaugh]] confirmation hearing, the personal information of senators [[Lindsey Graham]], [[Orrin Hatch]], and [[Mike Lee]] was posted on [[Wikipedia]] from an IP address traced to the [[United States Capitol|U.S. Capitol building]].

(01:29:05) Congressional staffer [[Jackson Cosko]], who had worked for Democratic representatives [[Sheila Jackson Lee]], [[Maggie Hassan]], and [[Barbara Boxer]], was charged with doxing the senators by posting their addresses and phone numbers online.

(01:41:04) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that the intense opposition to [[Brett Kavanaugh]] was primarily driven by the belief that he would vote to overturn ''Roe v. Wade'' and restrict abortion access.

(01:42:36) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] characterized the Democratic Party's political goals as fighting for increased immigration from [[Mexico]] and multiculturalism, while his side fought for the preservation of neighborhoods, families, culture, and inheritance.

(01:43:42) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] criticized [[Jeff Flake]] for attending the [[Global Citizen Festival]] and advocating for bipartisanship, calling him a "useful idiot."

(01:43:50) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] disputed [[Donald Trump]]'s statement that "we all bleed the same blood," arguing that political opponents and elites do not love [[United States|America]] or salute its flag.

(01:59:47) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] referenced a quote from [[Ben Shapiro]] about deep-seated ideological conflict preventing coexistence, which Shapiro had applied to the [[Israel]]-[[Palestine (region)|Palestine]] conflict.
