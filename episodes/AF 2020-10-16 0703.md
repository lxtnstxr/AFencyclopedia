## Summary

Immigration into the United States decreased by 97% in the third quarter of 2020 compared to the first quarter, representing the largest annual decline in U.S. history. FCC Chairman Ajit Pai announced the FCC would reinterpret Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act, a move Nicholas J. Fuentes argued could pressure social media companies to alter content moderation practices. Nielsen ratings indicated approximately 14 million viewers watched a Joe Biden town hall and 13.5 million watched a Donald Trump town hall, following the Commission on Presidential Debates' cancellation of a scheduled debate.

---

(00:00:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] announced a "casual Friday" episode of his show, focusing on a drastic reduction in immigration and developments at the [[Federal Communications Commission|FCC]].

(00:01:00) [[Patrick Casey]] had previously given [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] a hard time about projections for reduced legal immigration in fiscal years 2020 and 2021.

(00:01:25) A report from the [[Cato Institute]] showed that immigration into the [[United States]] had decreased by 97% in the third quarter of 2020 compared to the first quarter.

(00:02:05) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that the 97% reduction in immigration was evidence that [[Donald Trump]] was following through on his promises.

(00:02:28) The reduction in immigration in 2020 represented the biggest decrease in [[United States|American]] history and the lowest number of immigrants since 1965.

(00:03:06) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated that the show would discuss [[Federal Communications Commission|FCC]] Chairman [[Ajit Pai]], who had previously shut down Net Neutrality.

(00:03:50) [[Ajit Pai]] announced that the [[Federal Communications Commission|FCC]] would begin to reinterpret Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act, which [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued could stop major tech companies from censoring conservatives.

(00:06:10) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] covered a [[Donald Trump|Trump]] town hall on [[NBC]] where [[Savannah Guthrie]] interviewed the former president.

(00:06:25) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] also covered about a half-hour of a [[Joe Biden]] town hall hosted by [[George Stephanopoulos]].

(00:07:27) Nielsen ratings indicated approximately 14 million people watched the [[Joe Biden]] town hall and 13.5 million watched the [[Donald Trump|Trump]] town hall, compared to 83 million viewers for the first presidential debate.

(00:08:13) The [[Commission on Presidential Debates]] cancelled a scheduled debate after the [[Donald Trump|Trump]] campaign refused to participate in a virtual format due to coronavirus concerns.

(00:09:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] noted that the setups for the separate [[Donald Trump|Trump]] and [[Joe Biden]] town halls in [[Florida]] and [[Pennsylvania]] were socially distanced, making the cancellation of an in-person debate seem arbitrary.

(00:11:03) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] characterized [[Savannah Guthrie]]'s interview with [[Donald Trump]] as brutal and lacking impartiality, focusing on scandals like the [[White House]] event being a "super spreader" and accusations of white supremacy.

(00:12:30) In contrast, [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated that [[George Stephanopoulos]] did not interrupt [[Joe Biden]] during his town hall, which featured questions [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] considered soft, such as "what does it say about the country if Trump wins?"

(00:13:06) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] pointed out that the [[Joe Biden]] town hall occurred within 24 hours of the [[New York Post]] obtaining emails about [[Hunter Biden]] and [[Ukraine]], yet [[Joe Biden]] faced no questions about it.

(00:14:27) A second presidential debate was scheduled for October 22nd, which [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] planned to cover.

(00:15:16) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that [[Donald Trump]] lost a crucial opportunity to reach a large audience by not having a debate, which he needed as early voting numbers were concerning.

(00:16:00) [[Ryan Girdusky]] tweeted data showing that in [[Florida]], only 4% of newly registered black voters had registered as [[Republican Party|Republicans]], which [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] cited as evidence that outreach to black voters by figures like [[Candace Owens]] had failed.

(00:16:59) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] blamed [[Jared Kushner]] and [[Brad Parscale]] for an incompetent campaign strategy that focused on pandering to black voters through policies like the First Step Act and historically black college funding.

(00:17:53) Early voting numbers showed [[Republican Party|Republicans]] were behind in key states like [[Florida]], [[Pennsylvania]], and [[Iowa]].

(00:19:33) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] predicted the election would come down to [[Minnesota]], [[Wisconsin]], [[Michigan]], [[Pennsylvania]], and [[Florida]], with particular focus on the latter three.

(00:20:06) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that demographic trends showed swing states like [[New Mexico]], [[Nevada]], [[Colorado]], and [[Virginia]] becoming solidly [[Democratic Party|Democrat]], while [[Republican Party|Republican]]-leaning states like [[Texas]], [[Georgia]], and [[Arizona]] were becoming toss-ups.

(00:22:04) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated that [[California]] would remain a [[Democratic Party|Democrat]] state, while [[Texas]] was at risk of becoming a swing state.

(00:22:45) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] attributed the leftward shift of states largely to immigration and other demographic factors.

(00:23:24) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] noted that until recently, tech censorship was the only major issue [[Donald Trump]] had not addressed effectively in the final stretch of his reelection campaign.

(00:24:33) [[Federal Communications Commission|FCC]] Chairman [[Ajit Pai]] moved to revise guidance on Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act, which [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] described as the primary tactic to force companies like [[Facebook]] and [[Twitter]] to stop censoring conservatives.

(00:25:07) Section 230 of the 1996 Communications Decency Act provides social media platforms with legal immunity for user content, contingent on them being conducive to political discourse.

(00:26:45) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that if platforms like [[Facebook]] and [[Twitter]] make editorial decisions by censoring political opinions, they should be classified as publishers like the [[New York Times]] and lose their special legal immunity.

(00:29:05) An article from [[The Verge]] stated that [[Ajit Pai]] said the [[Federal Communications Commission|FCC]] would seek to regulate social media platforms at the behest of an executive order signed by [[Donald Trump]].

(00:29:36) [[Ajit Pai]] argued that social media companies have a [[First Amendment]] right to free speech but not a right to a special immunity denied to other media outlets like newspapers and broadcasters.

(00:31:02) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] used [[Fox News]] and [[Tucker Carlson]] as an example of a publisher that is held legally accountable for the content it advances, unlike social media platforms with Section 230 immunity.

(00:32:06) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] contested free market arguments for protecting tech companies, stating that major corporations like [[Amazon]], [[Twitter]], and [[Facebook]] achieved market dominance through government subsidies, regulations, and legal loopholes.

(00:34:25) In May, [[Donald Trump]] signed an executive order targeting tech companies like [[Facebook]] and [[Google]] concerning Section 230, instructing the [[United States Department of Commerce|Commerce Department]] to draft a petition for the [[Federal Communications Commission|FCC]].

(00:35:06) [[Ajit Pai]] stated that the [[Federal Communications Commission|FCC]]'s General Counsel confirmed the agency had the legal authority to reinterpret Section 230, and he intended to move forward with a rule-making to clarify its meaning.

(00:37:07) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that clarifying Section 230 would force companies like [[Twitter]] and [[Facebook]] to choose between being platforms that cannot censor or publishers that are legally liable, creating a financial incentive to stop censoring conservatives.

(00:38:19) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated that controlling the distribution of information through the internet was the most important issue, as it allowed dissenting voices to reach a mass audience, unlike traditional media controlled by a small group.

(00:40:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] claimed that after the 2016 election, the establishment worked to "button up" the internet by shutting down platforms for dissidents like the [[Daily Stormer]].

(00:42:23) Concurrently with the [[Federal Communications Commission|FCC]] action, the [[United States Department of Justice|Department of Justice]] was pursuing an antitrust case against [[Google]].

(00:42:54) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued the trajectory of tech companies was towards totalitarianism, with [[Google]] rewriting history and making dissident content inaccessible, leaving only [[Anti-Defamation League|ADL]]-approved information.

(00:44:01) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] introduced a report from the [[Cato Institute]] showing that legal immigration in the second half of fiscal year 2020 fell by 92% from the first half, the largest annual decline in [[United States|U.S.]] history.

(00:45:44) The [[Cato Institute]] report stated that legal immigration almost wholly stopped in April and May 2020 after the [[United States Department of State|State Department]] closed consulates and [[Donald Trump]] issued a proclamation suspending new visa issuances for most immigrant categories.

(00:46:30) The annualized legal immigration rate from abroad fell to 0.03% of the [[United States|U.S.]] population, the lowest rate except for three years during [[World War II]] and one year during the [[Great Depression]].

(00:48:50) The 92% drop in the second half of fiscal year 2020 was larger than the 73% decline in 1915 coinciding with [[World War I]], the 70% decline in 1925 from Congress closing legal immigration from Europe, and 63% declines in 1931, 1942, and 1918.

(00:50:01) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that the coronavirus-driven reduction in immigration to near zero created a new negotiating starting point, shifting the onus to those who would argue to increase immigration back up.

(00:52:57) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated that with immigration near zero, the [[America First]] movement should become prominent in the [[Republican Party]] to argue for keeping immigration low and dictating a new, restrictive policy.

(00:55:11) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] listed [[Donald Trump]]'s accomplishments as including a 97% reduction in immigration, the [[Federal Communications Commission|FCC]] moving against tech censorship, 450 miles of border wall construction, and troop reductions in [[Iraq]] and [[Afghanistan]].

(00:56:27) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] credited these accomplishments to [[Donald Trump]] overcoming opposition from [[Democratic Party|Democrats]], the media, intelligence agencies, and subversion from within his own party from figures like [[Paul Ryan]] and [[Mitch McConnell]].
