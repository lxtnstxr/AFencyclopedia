## Summary

Dianne Feinstein stated Christine Ford might not testify before the Senate Judiciary Committee regarding Brett Kavanaugh's confirmation. Donald Trump delivered a United Nations speech outlining a foreign policy agenda focused on Iran, North Korea, and a rejection of globalism. The Trump administration abandoned the goal of regime change in Syria, where James Mattis announced a continued U.S. troop presence. Iran blamed the United Arab Emirates, Saudi Arabia, Israel, and the United States for a terrorist attack in Ahvaz.

---

(00:21:00) [[Dianne Feinstein]] stated that [[Christine Ford]], the primary accuser in the [[Brett Kavanaugh]] Supreme Court confirmation hearings, might not testify before the Senate Judiciary Committee as scheduled.

(00:29:00) [[Michael Avenatti]], the attorney representing one of [[Brett Kavanaugh]]'s accusers, locked his Twitter account after publicly promoting a third, unverified accuser.

(00:42:00) [[Donald Trump]] delivered a 35-minute address to the [[United Nations]] General Assembly in [[New York City]], outlining a foreign policy agenda covering [[North Korea]], [[Syria]], [[Iran]], trade, and a rejection of globalism.

(00:55:00) Major media outlets including [[Fox News]], [[Reuters]], the [[Associated Press]], and the [[BBC]] characterized [[Donald Trump]]'s [[United Nations]] speech as being primarily focused on [[Iran]].

(01:11:00) [[Barack Obama]] reportedly warned [[Donald Trump]] that [[North Korea]] represented the greatest foreign policy challenge facing his incoming administration.

(01:15:00) [[Donald Trump]]'s administration pivoted U.S. foreign policy focus from the Middle East to Asia, prioritizing diplomatic engagement with [[North Korea]] over the [[Iran]] nuclear deal negotiated by the previous administration.

(01:31:00) [[James Mattis]], the U.S. Secretary of Defense, announced that approximately 2,000 American troops and 5,000 contractors would remain in northeastern [[Syria]] indefinitely.

(01:34:00) The foreign policy goal of the [[Barack Obama]] administration in [[Syria]] was regime change, involving training and arming rebels, conducting airstrikes, and maintaining a military presence to oppose [[Bashar al-Assad]].

(01:57:00) [[Donald Trump]]'s administration officially abandoned the goal of regime change in [[Syria]] in April 2018, shifting the stated objective to de-escalating the military conflict.

(02:18:00) [[Donald Trump]] threatened military retaliation against the [[Bashar al-Assad]] regime in [[Syria]] if chemical weapons were deployed, a stance maintained despite earlier intelligence warnings of an imminent attack in Idlib that did not materialize.

(02:29:00) The [[United States]] conducted two limited military strikes in [[Syria]] in response to alleged chemical weapons attacks: a missile strike on an airfield launched from the eastern Mediterranean and a later strike involving approximately 100 missiles that targeted three buildings.

(02:49:00) [[Donald Trump]]'s stated rationale for maintaining a troop presence in [[Syria]] included protecting the security interests of [[Turkey]], a [[NATO]] ally; supporting the rebuilding of [[Iraq]]; and safeguarding the security apparatus connected to [[Israel]].

(03:13:00) [[Donald Trump]] asserted that [[Iran]] had approximately 250,000 personnel on its payroll within [[Syria]], including members of the [[Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps]] and foreign militiamen, whom he identified as the root cause of the conflict.

(03:20:00) [[Donald Trump]] declared that the [[United States]] could not allow [[Iran]], which he labeled the world's leading sponsor of terrorism and a regime that chants "death to America" and threatens [[Israel]] with annihilation, to obtain nuclear weapons.

(03:46:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that [[Iran]]'s support for groups like [[Hamas]], [[Hezbollah]], and [[Palestinian Islamic Jihad]] constituted funding for an asymmetrical uprising in [[Palestine]], distinct from the Sunni terrorism funded by Gulf states like [[Qatar]] and the [[United Arab Emirates]].

(04:29:00) [[Donald Trump]] called on all nations to isolate the regime in [[Iran]] and to support the Iranian people in their struggle, rhetoric that some analysts interpreted as a potential prelude to calls for regime change.

(04:53:00) [[Donald Trump]] outlined a foreign policy doctrine he termed "principled realism," which he positioned as a rejection of the "democratic realism" advocated by figures like [[Charles Krauthammer]] and associated with the administrations of [[George H. W. Bush]], [[George W. Bush]], and [[Barack Obama]].

(05:19:00) [[Donald Trump]] explicitly rejected the ideology of globalism and embraced the doctrine of patriotism during his [[United Nations]] speech, framing it as a defense of American sovereignty against unelected global bureaucracies.

(05:38:00) [[Donald Trump]] announced that the [[United States]] would provide no support or recognition to the [[International Criminal Court]], which he declared had no jurisdiction, legitimacy, or authority over American citizens.

(06:10:00) [[Donald Trump]] used his [[United Nations]] speech to condemn socialism and communism, specifically criticizing the situation in [[Venezuela]] as an example of their failures.

(06:18:00) [[Emmanuel Macron]], the President of [[France]], used his own [[United Nations]] address to denounce nationalism, positioning it in direct opposition to [[Donald Trump]]'s advocacy for patriotism.

(06:25:00) Leaders in [[Hungary]] and [[Poland]], such as [[Viktor Orbán]], were defining their political movements in terms of nationalism versus globalism, according to [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]].

(06:48:00) A terrorist attack at a military parade in [[Ahvaz]], [[Iran]], killed 29 people, including members of the [[Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps]], with responsibility claimed by the militant group Al-Ahvaziya and the [[Islamic State of Iraq and the Levant|Islamic State]].

(07:08:00) The government of [[Iran]] blamed the [[United Arab Emirates]], [[Saudi Arabia]], [[Israel]], and the [[United States]] for the terrorist attack in [[Ahvaz]] and threatened a "crushing response."

(07:50:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] analyzed that the conflict between the [[United States]] and [[Iran]] had evolved into a 21st-century war involving political, informational, cyber, and asymmetrical guerrilla tactics, rather than conventional troop deployments.

(08:05:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] cited the [[Russia|Russian]] intervention in [[Ukraine]], where operatives without uniforms conducted operations, as an example of modern warfare tactics.

(08:29:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] explained that [[Russia]]'s strategic imperative to control [[Ukraine]] stemmed from the need for a geographic buffer along the Northern European Plain, a historic invasion route used by [[Napoleon Bonaparte|Napoleon]] and [[Nazi Germany]].

(08:55:00) Following the 2014 ousting of pro-Russian President [[Viktor Yanukovych]], [[Russia]] annexed [[Crimea]] and fomented rebellion in eastern [[Ukraine]] to prevent the country from joining [[NATO]] and the [[European Union]], according to [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]].

(09:02:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] contrasted the [[Monroe Doctrine]], which asserts [[United States|U.S.]] hegemony over the Western Hemisphere, with the denial of a similar sphere of influence for [[Russia]] in Eastern Europe.
