## Summary

Carlos Maza of Vox publicly accused Steven Crowder of fostering harassment, leading YouTube to demonetize Crowder's channel. YouTube subsequently expanded its hate speech policies, demonetizing creators including News2Share, James Allsup, and Jesse Lee Peterson. Commentators like Ben Shapiro and Tim Pool criticized YouTube's actions, which occurred amid broader antitrust probes by the Federal Trade Commission.

(00:00:05) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] began the 400th episode of his show, "America First," on a Wednesday evening, acknowledging a significant new development in the news.

(00:00:40) [[Carlos Maza]], a journalist for [[Vox]], initiated a new wave of demonetization on [[YouTube]], colloquially termed an "adpocalypse," which targeted numerous conservative commentators.

(00:01:21) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated his intention to detail the conflict between [[Carlos Maza]] and [[Steven Crowder]], including the subsequent policy changes by [[YouTube]] and the list of affected creators.

(00:08:12) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] characterized the ongoing events as a "second adpocalypse" instigated by [[Vox]], representing a broader systematic shift from a relatively unregulated "Internet 2.0" era to a more controlled and authoritarian online environment.

(00:11:32) The controversy originated on May 24th when [[Carlos Maza]] of [[Vox]] released an explainer video criticizing the media's relationship with [[Fox News]].

(00:13:27) On May 29th, [[Steven Crowder]] of "Louder with Crowder" released a rebuttal video to [[Carlos Maza]]'s content, escalating a long-standing feud where Crowder had previously used homophobic and ethnic slurs against Maza.

(00:14:17) [[Carlos Maza]] publicly accused [[Steven Crowder]] on [[Twitter]] of fostering years of harassment based on his sexual orientation and ethnicity through his videos and his fanbase.

(00:15:54) [[Steven Crowder]] argued that [[Carlos Maza]]'s campaign was a corporate tactic by the failing media company [[Vox]] to eliminate a successful competitor, "Louder with Crowder."

(00:17:32) [[Carlos Maza]] countered that the core issue was [[YouTube]]'s longstanding allowance of a major right-wing account to violate its policies and harass queer creators, specifically citing Crowder's sale of "Socialism is for fags" t-shirts.

(00:19:23) [[YouTube]] initially reviewed the flagged content and stated that while [[Steven Crowder]]'s language was hurtful, the videos did not violate its policies and would remain on the platform.

(00:22:23) Following continued pressure from [[Carlos Maza]] and his supporters, [[YouTube]] reversed its decision and suspended the monetization of [[Steven Crowder]]'s channel, citing a pattern of egregious actions harmful to the community.

(00:24:45) [[YouTube]] announced a significant policy change, expanding its hate speech rules to prohibit content alleging a group's superiority to justify discrimination and to remove content denying well-documented violent events like the Holocaust or the Sandy Hook shooting.

(00:28:37) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] listed creators demonetized as a result of the new policy, including [[News2Share]], [[The Golden One]], [[James Allsup]], [[Red Elephants]], [[Jesse Lee Peterson]], [[Martin Sellner]], [[Steven Crowder]], [[Red Ice TV]], and the left-wing podcast [[Drunken Peasants]].

(00:29:42) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] reported that accounts like [[Thulean Perspective]] (Varg Vikernes) and [[Zurious Music]] had been removed, while [[Gavin McInnes]], [[American Renaissance]], and [[Owen Benjamin]] had specific videos deleted.

(00:30:50) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] noted that commentators across the political spectrum, including [[Ben Shapiro]], [[Dan Bongino]], and [[Tim Pool]], were publicly criticizing [[YouTube]]'s actions.

(00:31:51) Despite [[YouTube]]'s demonetization of [[Steven Crowder]] and its new policies, [[Carlos Maza]] expressed continued dissatisfaction, demanding further action.

(00:32:31) [[YouTube]] clarified to [[Carlos Maza]] that [[Steven Crowder]] would need to remove links to his "Socialism is for fags" t-shirts and address all issues with his channel to be considered for reinstatement.

(00:33:33) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that the demonetization strategy was a more pernicious form of censorship than outright bans, as it silently crippled creators' livelihoods without generating equivalent public outcry.

(00:34:01) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] connected the [[YouTube]] controversy to broader antitrust probes by the [[Federal Trade Commission]] and the [[U.S. Department of Justice]] into major tech companies like [[Apple]], [[Amazon]], [[Facebook]], and [[Google]].

(00:40:02) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] reported that Democratic congresspersons had threatened to review [[YouTube]] and [[Facebook]]'s Section 230 legal protections if the companies did not more aggressively remove "hateful white nationalist content."

(00:43:14) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] framed the multi-year campaign of deplatforming figures like [[Alex Jones]], [[Laura Loomer]], and [[Milo Yiannopoulos]] as a systematic effort by major tech companies, motivated by post-2016 election vows, to prevent another political upset like [[Donald Trump]]'s victory.

(00:44:43) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] asserted that the ultimate goal of [[YouTube]], [[Google]], and the [[Democratic Party]] was absolute control over political discourse, targeting even small creators to ensure all influential voices were part of the established media conglomerate system.
