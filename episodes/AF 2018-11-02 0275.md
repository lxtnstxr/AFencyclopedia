## Summary

Patrick J. Buchanan served as a senior advisor to Presidents Richard Nixon, Gerald Ford, and Ronald Reagan and authored 13 books. Nicholas J. Fuentes contrasted paleoconservatives like Buchanan with neoconservatives such as Bill Kristol and Ben Shapiro. Fuentes stated that Ben Shapiro criticized Buchanan's column questioning the statement "all men are created equal" following the Charlottesville rally. Fuentes also reported that YouTube demonetized the channel of YouTuber Ralph Retort and refunded $27,000 in his charitable donations to St. Jude Children's Research Hospital.

(00:00:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] announced the show's topic for the evening would be the 80th birthday of [[Patrick J. Buchanan]] and an analysis of paleoconservatism versus neoconservatism.

(00:03:11) [[Patrick J. Buchanan]] served as a senior advisor to presidents [[Richard Nixon]], [[Gerald Ford]], and [[Ronald Reagan]] before running presidential campaigns in 1992, 1996, and 2000.

(00:04:44) [[Patrick J. Buchanan]] authored 13 books, including *Death of the West*, and co-founded [[The American Conservative]] magazine.

(00:05:33) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated that the conservative movement had been dominated for 30 years by neoconservatives, whom he contrasted with paleoconservatives like [[Patrick J. Buchanan]].

(00:06:52) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] identified [[Bill Kristol]] and the [[Weekly Standard]] as key figures in the rise of neoconservatism, which he said sidelined paleoconservatives after the 1990s.

(00:07:13) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] claimed [[William F. Buckley Jr.]] helped purge figures like [[Patrick J. Buchanan]] from the conservative movement.

(00:10:48) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] read from a column [[Patrick J. Buchanan]] wrote after the Charlottesville rally, in which Buchanan questioned the ideological foundation of the statement "all men are created equal."

(00:13:41) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] read a response by [[Ben Shapiro]] that criticized [[Patrick J. Buchanan]]'s column, arguing that "all men are created equal" is a description of common human value in the eyes of God and the law.

(00:15:55) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] recalled that [[Ben Shapiro]] once tweeted at him, invoking [[Patrick J. Buchanan]] in a criticism of Fuentes's views on free trade or [[Israel]].

(00:18:52) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] asserted that [[Ben Shapiro]]'s website, the [[Daily Wire]], is owned and funded by billionaires like the [[Wilks brothers]].

(00:19:27) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] referenced a [[PragerU]] video in which [[Dennis Prager]] argued that the [[United States]] was founded on values, not race or ethnicity.

(00:22:53) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] presented a whiteboard comparison characterizing neoconservatives as ethnically Jewish, Israel-first, pro-free trade, pro-war, profit-driven, and agents of the devil, while characterizing paleoconservatives as Christian, white, America-first, protectionist, non-interventionist, demography-focused, and aligned with God.

(00:23:22) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] listed [[Bret Stephens]], [[Max Boot]], [[Ben Shapiro]], [[Bill Kristol]], [[Charles Krauthammer]], and [[David Horowitz]] as examples of Jewish neoconservative intellectuals.

(00:23:48) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] claimed that figures like [[Paul Wolfowitz]], [[Douglas Feith]], and [[Richard Perle]] had worked for [[Israel]] in the 1990s.

(00:24:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] identified [[Patrick J. Buchanan]] and the writers at [[The American Conservative]] magazine as predominantly Catholic paleoconservatives.

(00:25:03) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that [[Ben Shapiro]] supports [[Israel]] as a nation for Jews but opposes America being a nation for white people, which Shapiro justifies by calling Israel an ideological nation.

(00:26:25) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated that [[Patrick J. Buchanan]]'s campaign slogan was "America First," a phrase [[Ben Shapiro]] and other Jewish figures have called anti-Semitic.

(00:27:02) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] asserted that the [[American Israel Public Affairs Committee|AIPAC]] and the neoconservative push for wars in [[Iraq]], [[Libya]], [[Afghanistan]], [[Yemen]], and proposed wars in [[Iran]] and [[Syria]] were done to benefit [[Israel]].

(00:28:04) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated that neoconservatives like [[Ben Shapiro]], [[George W. Bush]], and [[Mitt Romney]] favor free trade and oppose tariffs, in contrast to paleoconservative protectionism.

(00:29:37) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] referenced an article [[Ben Shapiro]] wrote on March 12, 2003, criticizing [[Patrick J. Buchanan]] and [[The American Conservative]] magazine for opposing the [[Iraq War]] and discussing the [[Israel lobby]].

(00:39:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] announced he would cover the upcoming midterm election results live on his next show with commentators [[Bryden]], [[Jazz Hands]], [[McFeels]], and an invited [[Red Elephants]].

(00:40:12) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] discussed the censorship of YouTuber [[Ralph Retort]], whose channel was demonetized and banned by [[YouTube]] after a [[Wall Street Journal]] reporter contacted him for a potential hit piece.

(00:42:40) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] reported that [[YouTube]] refunded $27,000 in charitable donations [[Ralph Retort]] had raised for [[St. Jude Children's Research Hospital]] during a charity stream.

(00:43:48) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated that the post-Charlottesville censorship campaign began with the removal of the [[Daily Stormer]], [[Milo Yiannopoulos]], [[Chuck Johnson]], and [[Andrew Anglin]], and later included [[Gavin McInnes]].

(00:46:24) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that [[Patrick J. Buchanan]] could only publish his books and appear on programs like [[Laura Ingraham]]'s and [[Sean Hannity]]'s because he was established; a new commentator with his views would be censored today.

(00:47:14) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] concluded that stopping political censorship by tech oligarchs would require state action, such as repealing Section 230 protections or pursuing antitrust legislation against companies like [[Google]].
