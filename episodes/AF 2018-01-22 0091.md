## Summary

The United States Senate and House of Representatives passed a stopgap funding measure, averting a government shutdown. Turkey launched a military operation into Syria's Afrin region against the Kurdish YPG, which controls significant territory along the border. During the Soviet-Afghan War, the United States provided support to Afghan mujahideen factions, some elements of which later formed Al-Qaeda and the Taliban.

(00:01:27) The [[United States Senate]] passed a stopgap measure to fund the government by a vote of 81 to 18, which subsequently passed the [[United States House of Representatives]] and was set to be signed by the president later in the week.

(00:08:38) [[Donald Trump]] rescinded the Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals (DACA) program via executive order in September 2017, a program originally established by [[Barack Obama]] through executive order after failing to secure congressional approval.

(00:10:29) During the government shutdown, [[Donald Trump]], [[Mitch McConnell]], and [[Paul Ryan]] presented a unified message blaming the [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democratic Party]] for the shutdown over their demands for DACA protections.

(00:11:08) The continuing resolution passed by the [[United States House of Representatives]] included funding for the Children's Health Insurance Program (CHIP) and missile defense, which Republicans argued Democrats were holding hostage for DACA.

(00:15:45) The [[United States Senate]] passed a three-week stopgap funding measure on the afternoon of January 22nd, setting a new deadline of February 8th for a long-term budget and DACA solution, after [[Mitch McConnell]] promised Democrats negotiations on DACA.

(00:23:12) [[Luis Gutiérrez]], a Congressman from [[Illinois]], stated he would be willing to support funding for a border wall in exchange for legal protections for DACA recipients, marking a shift from previous Democratic rhetoric.

(00:31:05) [[Turkey]] launched a military incursion into the Afrin region of [[Syria]] on January 22nd, targeting the Kurdish People's Protection Units (YPG), which Turkey considers linked to the terrorist Kurdistan Workers' Party (PKK).

(00:36:42) [[Bashar al-Assad]] has been the president of [[Syria]] since the year 2000, leading pro-government forces backed by [[Iran]], [[Russia]], and [[China]] during the Syrian Civil War.

(00:38:45) The Kurdish People's Protection Units (YPG), backed by the [[United States]], seized significant territory in northeastern [[Syria]] during the conflict against the Islamic State (ISIS), controlling approximately 60% of the Syria-Turkey border.

(00:41:09) The Kurdistan Workers' Party (PKK), designated a terrorist organization by [[Turkey]], has conducted attacks within [[Turkey]] for over 30 years and is considered by Turkey to be allied with the YPG in [[Syria]].

(00:46:01) During the Soviet-Afghan War in the 1980s, the [[United States]] provided weapons and support, including through the [[Central Intelligence Agency|CIA]], to Afghan mujahideen factions, some of which later formed the basis of [[Al-Qaeda]] and the [[Taliban]].

(00:49:40) [[David Ben-Gurion]], Israel's first Prime Minister, wrote journal entries in the 1950s outlining strategic plans for securing [[Israel]]'s regional interests, which some analysts reference in discussions of Middle Eastern conflicts.

(00:59:51) American tennis player Tennys Sandgren faced media questions about retweeting [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] after advancing to the quarterfinals of the Australian Open, with journalists labeling Fuentes a "white nationalist" who attended the Charlottesville rally.

(01:00:09) [[Oswald Mosley]], leader of the British Union of Fascists, was criticized for adopting overt fascist aesthetics like black uniforms and Roman salutes, which failed to gain traction in the liberal, individualist political culture of [[United Kingdom|Great Britain]].

(01:04:04) Political commentator Jacob Wohl argued that criticism of [[Israel]] often stems from "outright Jew hatred," a charge [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] rejected, stating his position is that [[United States|America]] should have the same right to sovereignty and self-interest as [[Israel]].

(01:07:17) During a debate, Jacob Wohl denied the existence of [[Israel]]'s nuclear program and downplayed the significance of Israeli espionage in the [[United States]], while also admitting his personal bias as a Jewish person in favor of [[Israel]].
