## Summary

Elizabeth Warren and Bill de Blasio were the only candidates in the first 2020 Democratic primary debate to support eliminating private health insurance. Julian Castro advocated for decriminalizing illegal border crossings and challenged Beto O'Rourke on the issue, with Cory Booker agreeing. Cory Booker stated he would not re-sign the Iran nuclear deal, while other candidates indicated they would.

(00:07:30) The first 2020 Democratic presidential primary debate, featuring [[Cory Booker]], [[Julian Castro]], [[Elizabeth Warren]], [[Beto O'Rourke]], [[Amy Klobuchar]], [[Tim Ryan]], [[Bill de Blasio]], [[John Delaney]], [[Tulsi Gabbard]], and [[Jay Inslee]], was held on [[NBC]] and moderated by [[Chuck Todd]] and [[Rachel Maddow]].

(00:08:46) During the debate, [[Elizabeth Warren]] and [[Bill de Blasio]] were the only candidates to raise their hands in support of a policy to completely eliminate private health insurance.

(00:09:32) [[Tim Ryan]] stated that the perception of the [[Democratic Party]] was that it was elitist, coastal, and Ivy League, and argued the party needed to reorient itself towards the [[Midwest]] and working people.

(00:11:52) [[Julian Castro]] advocated for decriminalizing illegal border crossings by making them a civil offense rather than a criminal violation under Section 1325 of the Immigration and Nationality Act.

(00:12:44) [[Julian Castro]] directly challenged [[Beto O'Rourke]] during the debate for not supporting the repeal of Section 1325 to decriminalize illegal immigration.

(00:17:28) [[Bill de Blasio]] criticized [[Beto O'Rourke]]'s healthcare position during an exchange, arguing that supporting a public option while preserving private insurance was insufficient.

(00:19:40) [[Julian Castro]] described abortion access as "reproductive justice" and called for protecting the reproductive rights of transgender people, a statement that received a large applause from the debate audience.

(00:25:26) [[Cory Booker]] agreed with [[Julian Castro]]'s position on decriminalizing illegal immigration during the debate.

(00:25:56) When asked about the Iran nuclear deal, only [[Cory Booker]] stated he would not re-sign the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action, while other candidates on stage indicated they would.

(00:27:15) [[Elizabeth Warren]] proposed treating gun violence as a public health emergency and called for more research on guns as her primary policy response during the debate.

(00:30:48) [[Elizabeth Warren]] and [[Jay Inslee]] argued that addressing climate change through green energy investment represented a major economic opportunity and a source of future jobs.

(00:32:26) [[Tulsi Gabbard]] and [[Tim Ryan]] engaged in a notable exchange on foreign policy regarding the war in [[Afghanistan]].

(00:33:10) A tally of speaking time during the debate showed [[Cory Booker]] spoke 11 times, [[Julian Castro]] 10 times, [[Elizabeth Warren]] 9 times, [[Beto O'Rourke]] 9 times, and [[Amy Klobuchar]] 8 times.

(00:34:50) [[Amy Klobuchar]] pitched her candidacy in her closing statement by emphasizing her electability, competence, and status as a non-establishment figure who could pass legislation.

(00:36:42) [[Cory Booker]]'s closing statement relied on boilerplate rhetoric about fighting for values and unity, which [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] compared unfavorably to the more defined messages of other candidates.

(00:42:24) [[Beto O'Rourke]] opened his participation in the debate by delivering a message entirely in Spanish.

(00:43:00) [[Bill de Blasio]] cited having a Black son named Dante as part of his personal narrative during the debate, and [[Cory Booker]] later referred to himself as "a Black man" in a subsequent exchange.

(00:50:32) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] announced he would provide live commentary for the second Democratic primary debate on [[DLive]] the following night, featuring [[Joe Biden]], [[Bernie Sanders]], [[Pete Buttigieg]], [[Kamala Harris]], and [[Andrew Yang]].
