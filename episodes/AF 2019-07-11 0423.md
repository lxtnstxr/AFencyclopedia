## Summary

The White House hosted a social media summit featuring conservative figures Benny Johnson, Charlie Kirk, and Jim Hoft. The Trump administration abandoned its effort to add a citizenship question to the 2020 census after a Supreme Court ruling and legal challenges. Donald Trump criticized Bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies as volatile and based on "thin air." Nicholas J. Fuentes argued that social media censorship was a critical political issue and characterized the White House summit as a placating gesture.

---

(00:01:17) The [[White House]] hosted a social media summit featuring conservative figures including [[Benny Johnson]], [[Charlie Kirk]], and [[Jim Hoft]] of [[Gateway Pundit]] to discuss alleged bias against conservatives by companies like [[Facebook]], [[Google]], and [[Twitter]].

(00:02:29) [[Josh Hawley]] and [[Matt Gaetz]] attended and spoke at the [[White House]] social media summit, which [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] considered an encouraging sign.

(00:02:50) The [[Donald Trump|Trump]] administration announced it would not include a citizenship question on the 2020 census, reversing a previous policy initiative.

(00:03:02) [[Donald Trump|Trump]] had intended to reinstate a citizenship question to the census, a question that had been present throughout most of [[United States]] history until removed by the [[Barack Obama|Obama]] administration in 2010.

(00:03:32) Chief Justice [[John Roberts]] ruled the [[Donald Trump|Trump]] administration's rationale for adding a citizenship question to the census was insufficient, requiring a new justification.

(00:04:24) The [[Donald Trump|Trump]] administration stated it would attempt to collect citizenship data through other executive branch means instead of the census, with the goal of potentially influencing congressional districting and electoral vote apportionment on a state-by-state basis.

(00:11:37) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that social media censorship is the most critical political issue because it directly impacts the ability to disseminate messages, win elections, and enact policy changes on issues like immigration.

(00:15:03) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] claimed that the majority of attendees at the [[White House]] social media summit, such as [[Bill Mitchell]], [[Jim Hoft]], [[Charlie Kirk]], and [[Benny Johnson]], had not experienced meaningful censorship and were controlled opposition.

(00:17:53) Cartoonist [[Ben Garrison]] was disinvited from the [[White House]] social media summit after initially receiving an invitation, reportedly due to pressure from donors concerned about media and [[Anti-Defamation League|ADL]] criticism labeling him an anti-Semite.

(00:19:18) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] reported that donor [[Sheldon Adelson]] pressured the [[White House]] to disinvite [[Ben Garrison]] from the social media summit because Garrison had criticized globalist figures like [[George Soros]] and [[Jeffrey Epstein]].

(00:22:31) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] cited connections between [[Jeffrey Epstein]] and former [[Israel|Israeli]] Prime Minister [[Ehud Barak]] and the [[Mossad]] as an example of overlapping interests between alleged left-wing globalists and right-wing Zionist actors.

(00:26:01) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] characterized the [[White House]] social media summit as a gesture to placate the base and prevent mutiny among supporters, rather than a serious effort to craft legislation or regulation against tech censorship.

(00:32:05) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated that including a citizenship question on the census would depress turnout among illegal immigrants and prevent them from being counted for congressional representation and Electoral College apportionment, which he argued disproportionately benefits the [[Democratic Party]].

(00:34:57) The [[Anti-Defamation League|ADL]] filed a legal challenge against the [[Donald Trump|Trump]] administration's effort to add a citizenship question to the census, leading to the Supreme Court's intervention.

(00:35:33) Facing printing deadlines and legal injunctions, the [[Donald Trump|Trump]] administration abandoned its effort to add the citizenship question to the 2020 census.

(00:37:47) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that the administration's fallback plan to collect citizenship data through other agencies and advise states on redistricting would be ineffective and face legal challenges, representing a total retreat on the issue.

(00:38:53) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] contended that [[Donald Trump|Trump]] should have issued an executive order to delay the census until the legal challenge was resolved, rather than accepting the Supreme Court's ruling.

(00:42:12) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] blamed advisors like [[Jared Kushner]] and personnel from the [[Republican National Committee|RNC]] for the administration's retreat on the census citizenship question.

(00:42:57) A report from [[VDare]] indicated that 42.1 million foreign-born people in the [[United States]] account for at least 56 Electoral College votes, with [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] asserting this population overwhelmingly votes for the [[Democratic Party]].

(00:44:40) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] criticized figures like [[Charlie Kirk]] and [[Benny Johnson]] for focusing on short-term electoral victory in 2020 while ignoring long-term demographic changes that he argued would make states like [[Texas]], [[Arizona]], and [[Georgia]] non-competitive for the [[Republican Party]] in the future.

(00:45:35) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] characterized the administration's retreat on the census question as part of a pattern of using gestures and rationalizations to placate supporters without making substantive changes, similar to promises on wall construction and immigration raids.

(01:00:43) [[Owen Benjamin]] had previously created a video for [[Prager University]] in February, which [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] cited as evidence of Benjamin's establishment ties prior to his later presentation as an anti-establishment figure.
