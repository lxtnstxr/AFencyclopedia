## Summary

Ashley St. Clair of Human Events magazine apologized after a video showed her mocking Republican voters in Colorado for prioritizing immigration. Nicholas J. Fuentes accused Will Chamberlain and Raheem Kassam of being "Zio-con" grifters gatekeeping the conservative movement. A Quinnipiac University poll showed Joe Biden leading Donald Trump by 13 points nationally in a hypothetical 2020 matchup. Nicholas J. Fuentes linked Trump's declining poll numbers to unmet immigration promises and warned of a permanent Democratic shift in states like Texas due to demographic changes.

---

(00:00:45) [[Ashley St. Clair]], a writer for [[Human Events]] magazine, was exposed in a video for mocking [[Republican Party|Republican]] voters who prioritize immigration as their top issue, arguing that such focus was "identity politics" causing electoral losses.

(00:01:28) [[Ashley St. Clair]] challenged [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] to a debate on abortion after he criticized her opposition to the Alabama abortion bill, but she deleted the challenge after [[Will Chamberlain]] instructed her not to appear on his show.

(00:02:34) [[Will Chamberlain]], who had previously debated [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] on [[Israel]] approximately two years prior, intervened to prevent [[Ashley St. Clair]] from debating [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]], leading her to delete her challenge and block him.

(00:03:27) Following the aborted debate, [[Ashley St. Clair]] was featured in [[Human Events]] magazine alongside [[Will Chamberlain]], raising her profile before the release of the video criticizing immigration-focused Republicans.

(00:09:30) In the revealed video, [[Ashley St. Clair]] stated that the "right's losing elections because of this identity politics," specifically mocking [[Colorado]] Republicans for prioritizing immigration despite, in her view, having very few illegal immigrants in the state.

(00:11:49) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that the presence of "grifters"—individuals who pretend to be [[America First]] or nationalist conservatives for personal gain—within political organizations, media, and government is a systemic problem undermining the movement.

(00:16:49) [[Will Chamberlain]] took [[Ashley St. Clair]] under his wing as an apprentice following the controversy, and his business partner [[Raheem Kassam]] defended her by dismissing the backlash as "social media drama" in a tweet.

(00:17:01) [[Raheem Kassam]] tweeted in defense of [[Ashley St. Clair]], stating, "I never thought I'd be the voice of reason... there is just so much new right social media drama going on right now," and criticized those engaging in the controversy.

(00:20:38) [[Ashley St. Clair]] posted an apology tweet claiming she "mischaracterized" her views "in the heat of a debate" and affirmed support for building the wall, stopping all legal immigration, and reelecting [[Donald Trump]].

(00:24:17) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] accused [[Will Chamberlain]] and [[Raheem Kassam]] of being "Zio-con" grifters who engage in gatekeeping within the conservative movement to control influence and money, rather than advancing authentic conservative goals.

(00:28:42) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated that the backlash to his criticism of "Zio-cons" like [[Will Chamberlain]] came primarily from individuals concerned with defending the term rather than addressing the substantive accusation of grifting.

(00:30:07) [[Will Chamberlain]] defended [[Ashley St. Clair]] by citing her age, tweeting that she was 20 and asking critics to consider what she was going through, which [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] highlighted as hypocritical given Chamberlain's past blacklisting of him.

(00:31:18) [[Will Chamberlain]] had previously tweeted that he blacklisted [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] 18 months prior when he was 19 years old because [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] was an "anti-Semitic racist," a stance [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] contrasted with Chamberlain's defense of [[Ashley St. Clair]].

(00:33:59) A new [[Quinnipiac University]] poll reported by [[The Hill]] showed [[Joe Biden]] leading [[Donald Trump]] by 13 points nationally (53% to 40%) in a hypothetical 2020 matchup, with the poll's assistant director describing it as a "landslide margin."

(00:34:47) The [[Quinnipiac University]] poll revealed a massive gender gap, with [[Joe Biden]] leading [[Donald Trump]] 60% to 34% among women, while men were nearly evenly split (47% for Biden, 46% for Trump).

(00:35:30) The [[Quinnipiac University]] poll showed [[Donald Trump]] and [[Joe Biden]] virtually tied among white voters (47% to 46%), but Biden led 85% to 12% among black voters and 58% to 33% among Hispanic voters.

(00:36:04) Analyst [[Ryan Girdusky]] noted on Twitter that [[Donald Trump]]'s support among white voters had fallen from 57% in 2016 to 50% in the 2019 poll, and among non-college educated whites from 66% to 54%, indicating a significant erosion of his base.

(00:40:31) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that [[Donald Trump]]'s declining poll numbers were directly linked to his failure to deliver on his core immigration promises, noting that polls showed immigration was the number one issue for Republicans and independents.

(00:42:13) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] contended that "grifters" within the [[White House]] and conservative media were enabling [[Donald Trump]]'s presidency by giving him false assurances about his performance and policy priorities, leading to poor electoral prospects.

(00:43:15) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] warned of a "demographic winter" where non-white immigration would permanently shift states like [[Texas]], [[Arizona]], [[Nevada]], [[Colorado]], [[Florida]], and [[Georgia]] toward the [[Democratic Party]], making national Republican victories impossible.

(00:46:28) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] recalled the night of [[Donald Trump]]'s 2016 election victory, describing it as "pure ecstasy" and detailing how he and fellow Trump supporters celebrated in [[Boston]], visiting multiple election viewing parties and facing hostility from Clinton supporters.

(00:48:42) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated that after the 2016 election, he listened to the ten-hour version of the "Never Come Down" video featuring [[Donald Trump]] dancing on [[Saturday Night Live]], feeling that the political movement would "never come down ever again."

(00:58:42) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] expressed frustration with male "beta orbiters" or "reply guys" who defend figures like [[Ashley St. Clair]] online in a pathetic attempt to gain favor, calling the behavior "cringe" and sad.

(01:00:49) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] criticized new "free speech" platforms like [[Parler]] and [[Jordan Peterson]]'s proposed platform, arguing that existing alternatives like [[Gab]], run by [[Andrew Torba]], were sufficient and that new ventures were motivated by profit rather than principle.

(01:03:59) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] advised college students not to aggressively "red-pill" their peers on politics unless discussions arose naturally, warning that forcing the issue could damage personal and professional relationships with little chance of changing minds.

(01:04:50) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] affirmed his [[Catholic]] faith and its deontological ethical framework, while expressing skepticism about [[Immanuel Kant]]'s philosophy, which he suggested attempted to derive morality from rationalism outside of revelation.
