## Summary

The Wyoming State Republican Party censured Liz Cheney after United States House of Representatives Republican leadership declined to remove her from her position. Nicholas J. Fuentes announced the second America First Political Action Conference for February 26th in Orlando, implementing enhanced security measures including ticket sales via mailed checks or cash. Fuentes stated his show America First began on Right Side Broadcasting Network in February 2017 but was deplatformed after the Charlottesville rally, leading to bans from YouTube and DLive. Fuentes argued the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, via the Wall Street Journal and former director Thomas Frieden, stated COVID-19 would become an endemic disease requiring lasting behavioral modifications.

(00:30:20) The [[Wyoming State Republican Party]] censured [[Liz Cheney]] after the [[Republican Party]] leadership in the [[United States House of Representatives]] declined to remove her from her leadership position.

(00:33:40) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] announced that tickets for the second America First Political Action Conference (AFPAC 2) were on sale for February 26th in [[Orlando]], with general admission costing $100 and a pre-conference reception costing an additional $50.

(00:36:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated that AFPAC 2 would implement enhanced security measures, including selling tickets only via mailed checks or cash to a PO box to avoid third-party data systems, with the event location emailed to attendees on the day of the conference.

(00:39:40) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] credited an individual referred to as "Assistant Groyper" with working hundreds of hours to organize AFPAC 2.

(00:49:40) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] recounted that the first AFPAC in 2020 was an invite-only event for about 150-200 attendees and featured a speech by [[Michelle Malkin]].

(00:50:40) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated that his show, America First, began its first episode on [[Right Side Broadcasting Network]] on February 6, 2017, from a dorm room at [[Boston University]].

(00:52:20) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] described the early structure of his show as involving 45 minutes of monologue and 15 minutes of fielding questions from Twitter using the hashtag #AmericaFQ.

(00:56:40) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated he was deplatformed from the show's initial run on [[Right Side Broadcasting Network]] after the Charlottesville rally in August 2017, after which he started an independent YouTube channel.

(01:00:20) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] noted that he was banned from [[YouTube]] on Valentine's Day 2020 and later banned from [[DLive]], leading him to build his own streaming platform.

(01:04:20) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that the [[Centers for Disease Control and Prevention|CDC]] and scientists, via an article in the [[Wall Street Journal]], were stating that COVID-19 would become an endemic disease requiring lasting personal and societal behavioral modifications, including indefinite masking.

(01:05:40) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] cited former [[Centers for Disease Control and Prevention|CDC]] director [[Thomas Frieden]] as saying that lives would not return to normal and that long-term changes were necessary to coexist with COVID-19.

(01:06:20) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that lockdown measures could not be sustainable long-term and were predicated on vaccinating most of the world's population, a goal he deemed unattainable, meaning the restrictions would never end.

(01:09:20) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] claimed that the [[Biden administration]] was using the intolerable conditions of lockdown to later offer relief contingent on public compliance with government demands like vaccines, contact tracing spyware, and mask mandates, thereby equating basic freedoms with privileges granted by the state.

(01:11:40) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] pointed to riots in [[Europe]] as an example of public backlash against COVID-19 lockdowns that could potentially escalate to actions against governments.

(01:14:20) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] characterized the [[Wyoming State Republican Party]]'s censure of [[Liz Cheney]] as part of an "America First insurgency" and a sign of ongoing mass resistance against the [[Republican Party]] establishment, with similar actions potentially facing [[Tim Scott]], [[Lindsey Graham]], and [[Ben Sasse]] in their home states.

(01:15:40) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that the anti-establishment sentiment within local and state [[Republican Party]] organizations, fueled by actions like protecting [[Liz Cheney]], presented an opportunity for America First activists to infiltrate and guide the party toward a more muscular nationalist ideology.

(01:17:20) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated that [[Donald Trump]] either started or significantly advanced the America First political movement, but that completing its goals would require multi-generational effort and succession of leadership over a long-term horizon.
