## Summary

Nicholas J. Fuentes stated his initial political views were shaped by Milton Friedman and Thomas Sowell. Ian Fletcher argued the United States was founded as an explicitly protectionist country and authored the book *Free Trade Doesn't Work*. Fletcher identified the Coalition for a Prosperous America as his organization and stated Donald Trump's imposition of tariffs on China was sensible.

---

(00:00:05) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] hosted [[Ian Fletcher]] for an interview on his show.

(00:00:17) [[Ian Fletcher]] authored the book *Free Trade Doesn't Work*.

(00:00:49) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated that his initial political views were shaped by [[Milton Friedman]] and [[Thomas Sowell]].

(00:02:04) [[Ian Fletcher]] argued that the [[United States]] was founded as an explicitly protectionist country.

(00:02:11) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] agreed that [[Pat Buchanan]] also wrote about the historical success of tariffs during the Industrial Revolution.

(00:02:47) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] identified the [[American Enterprise Institute]], the [[Cato Institute]], and the [[Heritage Foundation]] as organizations pushing free trade dogma.

(00:03:24) [[Ian Fletcher]] clarified that the [[Cato Institute]] is libertarian and the [[American Enterprise Institute]] is pro-business, not conservative.

(00:05:23) [[Ian Fletcher]] argued that libertarian free trade ideology lacks a framework for prioritizing the good of the [[United States]] over global abstractions.

(00:05:51) [[Ian Fletcher]] stated that libertarians were defending the right of the dictatorship in [[Beijing]] to grow rich and arm itself at the expense of the [[United States]].

(00:06:53) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] noted that [[China]]'s exponential wealth growth over 25 years coincided with a lack of U.S. trade barriers and aggressive Chinese protections.

(00:07:27) [[Ian Fletcher]] argued that unilateral U.S. free trade is ineffective when trading partners like [[China]] maintain systems of subsidies and market distortions.

(00:08:45) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated that [[Donald Trump|Trump]] frequently highlighted the disparity in auto tariffs between the [[United States]] and [[China]].

(00:09:35) [[Ian Fletcher]] corrected a common free trade quote, attributing it to 19th-century French intellectual [[Frédéric Bastiat]] rather than [[Ronald Reagan]].

(00:09:48) [[Ian Fletcher]] pointed to [[China]]'s double-digit economic growth under a tariff-protected economy as evidence that tariffs are not inherently harmful.

(00:10:21) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] recalled that [[Paul Krugman]] argued insufficient stimulus prolonged the recession, a logic he compared to free trade defenses.

(00:11:42) [[Ian Fletcher]] explained that a persistent U.S. trade deficit finances imports through debt and asset sales, leading to deindustrialization.

(00:12:50) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] summarized [[Ian Fletcher]]'s argument that a trade deficit means the [[United States]] cannot get something for nothing and must pay via assets, currency, or debt.

(00:13:57) [[Ian Fletcher]] described free trade defenses as relying on conceptual "mumbo jumbo" and the fallacy of composition regarding trade deficits.

(00:15:58) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated that [[Ben Shapiro]] and other free trade proponents often argue a trade deficit with [[China]] is beneficial, analogous to buying groceries.

(00:16:35) [[Ian Fletcher]] dismissed the argument that a trade deficit is an "arbitrary accounting measure" as nonsensical, comparing it to calling a mortgage arbitrary.

(00:17:19) [[Ian Fletcher]] refuted the grocery store analogy for trade deficits, comparing it to running up credit card debt for immediate consumption at the expense of future wealth.

(00:18:35) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] referenced [[Ian Fletcher]]'s debunking of the myth that multinational corporations' location is irrelevant, noting most business is domestic.

(00:19:10) [[Ian Fletcher]] argued that for a prosperous modern economy, high-value, high-wage steps of the supply chain must occur within its territory.

(00:20:06) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] recalled that [[Barack Obama]] argued manufacturing jobs were not coming back and that America had moved past that stage of the economy.

(00:20:45) [[Ian Fletcher]] labeled the idea that manufacturing is unimportant as "hyper stupid," stating a developed country cannot exist without a strong manufacturing sector.

(00:21:30) [[Ian Fletcher]] cited [[Germany]] and [[Japan]] paying autoworkers more than the [[United States]] as evidence that high-wage nations can succeed in manufacturing.

(00:22:31) [[Ian Fletcher]] noted that [[General Motors]] went bankrupt while [[Toyota]] and [[Mercedes-Benz]] did not, despite higher wages in [[Germany]] and [[Japan]].

(00:23:57) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] summarized a theme from [[Ian Fletcher]]'s book that free trade optimizes for present consumption but mortgages long-term wealth and strategic capacity.

(00:24:41) [[Ian Fletcher]] agreed that free trade maximizes short-term consumption but erodes long-term productive capacity and national wealth.

(00:25:22) [[Ian Fletcher]] stated that the [[United States]] cannot be a military superpower without being an industrial superpower.

(00:26:29) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] compared free trade to communism, suggesting it sounds good in theory but fails in practical reality, especially regarding strategic challenges.

(00:26:43) [[Ian Fletcher]] disagreed, arguing that theoretical analyses exist to explain why free trade fails and why the historic U.S. tariff-protected strategy was successful.

(00:27:20) [[Ian Fletcher]] argued that pre-World War II Americans understood protectionism, but the [[United States]] opened its markets during the Cold War as a bribe to prevent [[Europe]] and [[Japan]] from going communist.

(00:28:58) [[Ian Fletcher]] stated that [[Donald Trump|Trump]]'s imposition of tariffs, particularly on [[China]], was sensible and an improvement, though he would have phased them in more gradually.

(00:29:41) [[Ian Fletcher]] identified fixing the overvalued U.S. dollar as a critical unaddressed issue for [[Donald Trump|Trump]]'s trade policy, as it makes exports too expensive and imports too cheap.

(00:30:15) [[Ian Fletcher]] revealed he has an indirect relationship with the [[Donald Trump|Trump]] administration and knows people who have spoken to the president.

(00:31:05) [[Ian Fletcher]] explained that the post-World War II system of fixed exchange rates was preferable to the current system of manipulated rates by trading partners like [[China]] and [[Germany]].

(00:31:53) [[Ian Fletcher]] proposed capital controls as a solution to manage the overvalued dollar and prevent large trade deficits.

(00:32:09) [[Ian Fletcher]] identified his organization as the [[Coalition for a Prosperous America]].

(00:33:20) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] asked [[Ian Fletcher]] to explain [[China]]'s currency manipulation and its damaging effects on the U.S. economy.

(00:34:14) [[Ian Fletcher]] explained the theoretical mechanism where a trade deficit should drive a currency down to balance trade, which [[China]] prevents by hoarding dollars.

(00:34:50) [[Ian Fletcher]] detailed [[China]]'s currency manipulation, where exporters must exchange dollars for yuan at state-controlled banks, and the government uses those dollars to buy U.S. assets like Treasury bills.

(00:35:44) [[Ian Fletcher]] stated that [[China]] has accumulated trillions of dollars in foreign assets through this process, creating demand that prevents the dollar from sinking as it should.

(00:37:14) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] noted that [[Donald Trump|Trump]] had levied tariffs on approximately $250 billion of Chinese goods and discussed adding more.

(00:37:58) [[Ian Fletcher]] stated that U.S. tariffs on [[China]] are meaningful and will drive some Chinese manufacturing to other countries like [[Vietnam]], the [[Philippines]], [[Malaysia]], and [[Indonesia]].

(00:38:36) [[Ian Fletcher]] expressed hope that [[Donald Trump|Trump]] would next address the overvaluation of the dollar, as it affects the trading relationship with the entire world, not just [[China]].

(00:39:23) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] observed that U.S. allies engage in protectionist practices as bad as or worse than [[China]]'s.

(00:40:03) [[Ian Fletcher]] stated that [[Canada]] is not a large economic problem for the [[United States]], and the trade dispute stemmed from the political flashpoint of [[NAFTA]].

(00:40:39) [[Ian Fletcher]] assessed the new [[NAFTA]] agreement as an improvement over the old one, though just a starting point.

(00:41:04) [[Ian Fletcher]] argued that most U.S. trade is not determined by trade agreements, and revaluing the dollar would be more impactful than renegotiating [[NAFTA]].

(00:42:43) [[Ian Fletcher]] praised the new [[NAFTA]] for mostly eliminating investor-state dispute settlement, an undemocratic system invented for countries without reputable courts.

(00:43:56) [[Ian Fletcher]] listed improvements in the new [[NAFTA]], including increased auto content requirements, minimum wage rules, and language on currency, labor, and environment, though enforcement was light.

(00:44:47) [[Ian Fletcher]] argued that undermining worker prosperity undermines conservative society, and he wanted enforceable labor standards in trade deals, primarily applying to [[Mexico]].

(00:45:22) [[Ian Fletcher]] suggested that enforcing high environmental standards in trade deals would be a strategic way to pressure [[China]], given its lower standards.

(00:46:03) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] noted that international climate agreements like the Paris Accords often squeeze the [[United States]] financially while giving [[China]] a pass.

(00:46:44) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] asked [[Ian Fletcher]] to evaluate the strategic argument for the Trans-Pacific Partnership to strengthen ties with Pacific countries and counter [[China]].

(00:47:38) [[Ian Fletcher]] dismissed the strategic argument for the TPP as "unbelievable nonsense," stating the [[United States]] defends allies like [[South Korea]] and should not bribe them for the privilege.

(00:48:24) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] agreed, noting the [[United States]] foots the bill for military presence in countries like [[Japan]] and [[South Korea]].

(00:49:24) [[Ian Fletcher]] stated that the primary goal of U.S. trade policy should be to eliminate the half-trillion-dollar annual trade deficit.

(00:50:16) [[Ian Fletcher]] explained the concept of mercantilism, showing how gaming the trade system has been profitable for centuries for countries like [[China]], [[Japan]], and [[Germany]].

(00:50:54) [[Ian Fletcher]] predicted the [[United States]] would need to maintain credible trade restrictions for the foreseeable future due to incentives for other nations to manipulate the system.

(00:52:32) [[Ian Fletcher]] graded [[Donald Trump|Trump]]'s trade policy an A-, and his handling of the broader economic relationship with the world a B+, which could improve with action on currency.

(00:53:04) [[Ian Fletcher]] directed viewers to his book at FreeTradeDoesntWork.com and his organization, the [[Coalition for a Prosperous America]], at ProsperousAmerica.org.

(00:53:31) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] concluded the interview with [[Ian Fletcher]], thanking him for his insights.
