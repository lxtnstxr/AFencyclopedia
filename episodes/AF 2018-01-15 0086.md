## Summary

Nicholas J. Fuentes argued the United States should cut all foreign aid to Israel, citing its nuclear program, espionage, and technology sales. Jacob Wohl contended U.S. aid to Israel provides strategic benefits, including intelligence returns and military access, while subsidizing American defense contractors. Fuentes referenced scholars John Mearsheimer and Stephen Walt, who argued Israel's region holds little strategic importance for the United States. Wohl stated Israel votes with the United States in the United Nations 94% of the time and has accepted two-state solutions rejected by Palestinian leaders.

(00:00:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] hosted a debate on American policy towards [[Israel]] with guest [[Jacob Wohl]].

(00:01:32) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that the [[United States]] should treat [[Israel]] like any other country and cut all foreign aid, citing [[Israel]]'s illegal nuclear program, espionage activities, and history of selling U.S. military technology to foreign countries.

(00:02:48) [[Jacob Wohl]] argued that U.S. foreign aid to [[Israel]], averaging three billion dollars annually, was a good investment as approximately 75% of the military-focused aid is spent within the [[United States]], subsidizing jobs at companies like [[Boeing]] and [[Raytheon]].

(00:05:35) [[Jacob Wohl]] stated that foreign aid allows the [[United States]] to exert influence over recipient countries, citing [[Donald Trump|Trump]]'s threat to cut aid to [[Pakistan]] as an example of this leverage.

(00:07:56) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] countered that [[Israel]], the largest recipient of U.S. foreign aid since 1978, conducts aggressive espionage against the [[United States]], steals technology, and expands settlements contrary to U.S. policy, demonstrating a lack of American influence.

(00:09:17) [[Jacob Wohl]] disputed that [[Israel]] is the top recipient of foreign aid, stating that [[Afghanistan]] has held that position and receives aid while producing heroin and Islamic terrorism.

(00:10:33) [[Jacob Wohl]] stated that the [[United States]] cooperates extensively with the [[Mossad]], receiving an estimated $50 to $60 billion worth of intelligence in exchange for $18 billion in aid over an 18-year period.

(00:12:08) [[Jacob Wohl]] cited the strategic benefits of U.S. aid to [[Israel]], including access to the port of [[Haifa]] and reliable airbases for refueling U.S. military aircraft in the Middle East.

(00:13:28) [[Jacob Wohl]] contrasted the U.S. relationship with [[Israel]], based on "soft power" and aid, with its relationships with [[Germany]] and [[South Korea]], which involve hard power and troop deployments following invasions or wars.

(00:16:35) [[Jacob Wohl]] argued that [[Israel]] has been open to a two-state solution on six separate occasions, but each time the Palestinians, including [[Mahmoud Abbas]], rejected the peace offers.

(00:17:54) [[Jacob Wohl]] attributed international criticism of [[Israel]]'s settlement policy to "unabashed Jew hatred," noting that no such criticism existed when [[Jordan]] and [[Egypt]] occupied the territories.

(00:18:49) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated his opposition was not to [[Israel]]'s actions but to funding them with U.S. aid, arguing that [[Israel]] deserves the most scrutiny as the number one recipient of foreign aid since 1978.

(00:20:26) [[Jacob Wohl]] cited [[Israel]]'s record of voting with the [[United States]] in the [[United Nations]] 94% of the time as evidence of a strong alliance.

(00:22:41) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] referenced foreign policy scholars [[John Mearsheimer]] and [[Stephen Walt]], who argued in their book *The Israel Lobby* that the Eastern Mediterranean region where [[Israel]] is located is not strategically important for the [[United States]].

(00:24:03) [[Jacob Wohl]] cited a statement by Major General [[George Keegan Jr.]] that between 1974 and 1990, the [[United States]] received $50 to $80 billion in intelligence and other benefits from [[Israel]] in exchange for $18.3 billion in military grants.

(00:25:08) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] quoted a former CIA official who characterized intelligence received from [[Israel]] as "laughably bad" and "gossip stuff," and stated it was never essential to U.S. military power.

(00:27:34) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that the influence of the [[American Israel Public Affairs Committee|AIPAC]], which two-thirds of Congress attends annually, explains why the [[United States]] continues to fund [[Israel]] despite receiving poor intelligence.

(00:28:48) [[Jacob Wohl]] defined [[Iran]] and [[North Korea]] as rogue states because their nuclear programs are explicitly intended to wipe the [[United States]] and its allies off the map.

(00:29:24) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] drew a parallel between [[Pakistan]], a nuclear state that supports terrorist groups, and [[Israel]], a nuclear state whose defense minister expressed a preference for [[ISIS]] control in [[Syria]] over Iranian proxies.

(00:30:06) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] cited statements by Israeli Defense Minister [[Moshe Ya'alon]] and a military intelligence chief indicating [[Israel]] did not want to see [[ISIS]] defeated in [[Syria]], a position he argued was opposite to U.S. policy under the [[Donald Trump|Trump]] administration.

(00:32:56) [[Jacob Wohl]] noted that [[Israel]] named a train station near the Western Wall after [[Donald Trump|Trump]] and that [[ISIS]] had been largely defeated during his administration, arguing these simultaneous events disproved the theory of an Israeli-[[ISIS]] alliance.

(00:35:21) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that [[Israel]]'s policy in [[Syria]] aimed to prevent a stable government in [[Damascus]] and retain control of the [[Golan Heights]], which was different from the U.S. goal of stabilizing the region to eradicate [[ISIS]].

(00:37:58) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] asserted that [[Israel]] possesses an illegal nuclear program, citing the 1965-1980 "Apollo Affair" investigation where CIA Deputy Director [[Carl Duckett]] believed missing highly enriched uranium had been diverted to [[Israel]].

(00:42:56) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] cited multiple sources, including a 2013 National Intelligence Estimate and the case of [[Larry Franklin]], to argue that [[Israel]] runs the third most aggressive espionage operation in the [[United States]] after [[Russia]] and [[China]].

(00:45:02) [[Jacob Wohl]] asked [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] if, as a Jewish person, he was prejudiced in favor of [[Israel]], to which [[Jacob Wohl]] replied, "Absolutely."

(00:45:14) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated that individuals with prejudices in favor of other countries, rather than the [[United States]], have no place in American politics, referencing the national motto "E pluribus unum."

(00:46:35) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] noted that more than half of the 25 neoconservative intellectuals who advocated for the war in [[Iraq]] were Jewish, implying a connection to their pro-[[Israel]] prejudice.

(00:52:41) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] presented evidence that the [[Iraq]] war was promoted for [[Israel]]'s benefit, quoting [[Philip Zelikow]] of the President's Foreign Intelligence Advisory Board and General [[Wesley Clark]], who said the real, unspoken threat was to [[Israel]].

(00:54:20) [[Jacob Wohl]] argued that blaming the [[Iraq]] war on "25 half-Jewish neoconservative intellectuals" was spurious, noting that the [[United Nations]] Security Council, Congress, and European nations all supported the invasion.

(00:55:04) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] cited post-war reviews which found that [[Israel]] provided false intelligence on [[Iraq]]'s WMD program and that Israeli officials publicly advocated for the war, demonstrating a shared interest in regional destabilization.

(01:01:56) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] read quotes from Israeli Prime Ministers [[David Ben-Gurion]] and [[Yitzhak Shamir]] to argue that [[Israel]] has a history of brutality and endorsement of terrorism, countering the claim that it is a "shining light" of morality.
