## Summary

Kamala Harris criticized Donald Trump's corporate tax cuts and launched a pre-planned attack on Joe Biden's past record on racial issues during the second 2019 Democratic primary debate. Joe Biden's odds of winning the nomination fell 3% in PredictIt markets following the debate, while Kamala Harris's odds surged 7%. Post-debate Google Trends data indicated Pete Buttigieg received the largest surge in search interest among the candidates.

---

(00:00:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] hosted live coverage and analysis of the second 2019 Democratic primary presidential debate on DLive, following a similar broadcast for the first debate the previous night.

(00:00:00) The second debate featured [[Joe Biden]], [[Bernie Sanders]], [[Pete Buttigieg]], [[Kamala Harris]], [[Michael Bennet]], [[Kirsten Gillibrand]], [[Marianne Williamson]], [[Andrew Yang]], [[Eric Swalwell]], and [[John Hickenlooper]].

(00:05:00) [[Kamala Harris]] delivered a strong opening statement criticizing the [[Donald Trump|Trump]] corporate tax cuts, framing them as a liability that primarily benefited corporations and the wealthiest one percent.

(00:07:00) The debate's first major topic shifted from the economy to healthcare, revealing a division within the [[Democratic Party]] between pragmatists and progressives over Medicare for All versus a public option.

(00:08:00) During the healthcare discussion, only [[Bernie Sanders]] and [[Kamala Harris]] supported completely abolishing private health insurance, while others like [[Pete Buttigieg]] and [[Kirsten Gillibrand]] advocated for more incremental approaches.

(00:12:00) On immigration, every candidate on stage raised their hand in support of decriminalizing illegal border crossings and providing government-funded healthcare to undocumented immigrants.

(00:14:00) [[Kamala Harris]] gently criticized [[Joe Biden]]'s immigration record from the [[Barack Obama|Obama]] administration, specifically citing deportations in [[California]] of immigrants without serious criminal offenses, to differentiate herself as more progressive.

(00:15:00) [[Michael Bennet]] invoked the Holocaust during a discussion on [[China]], though the topic was largely dismissed as unimportant within the broader debate.

(00:16:00) The most critical moment of the debate occurred during a discussion on criminal justice, where [[Kamala Harris]] launched a pre-planned, devastating attack on [[Joe Biden]]'s past record on racial issues, including his defense of segregationists and opposition to busing policies.

(00:18:00) [[Kamala Harris]] connected her attack on [[Joe Biden]] to her personal experience, stating she was a victim of the same segregationist busing policies he supported, highlighting their differences in age, race, gender, and political progressivism.

(00:20:00) Following [[Kamala Harris]]'s attack, [[Joe Biden]] appeared flustered and failed to recover for the remainder of the debate, cementing his status as the biggest loser of the night.

(00:21:00) On other issues, all candidates expressed support for abortion rights, and discussions on climate change and guns were perfunctory, with [[Eric Swalwell]] launching an ineffective attack on [[Bernie Sanders]]' gun control record.

(00:22:00) A tally of speaking time showed [[Bernie Sanders]] spoke the most (11 times), followed by [[Joe Biden]] (10 times), [[Kamala Harris]] (9 times), [[Pete Buttigieg]] (8 times), and [[Andrew Yang]] the least (2 times).

(00:24:00) Post-debate Google Trends data indicated [[Pete Buttigieg]] received the largest surge in search interest, followed by [[Andrew Yang]] and then [[Kamala Harris]], suggesting a significant boost in name recognition for Buttigieg.

(00:26:00) Betting market data from PredictIt showed [[Joe Biden]]'s odds of winning the nomination fell 3% to 21%, while [[Kamala Harris]] surged 7% to 19%, moving into second place alongside [[Elizabeth Warren]].

(00:28:00) PredictIt markets indicated 80% of bettors believed [[Joe Biden]]'s polling would decline after the debate, while 91% believed [[Kamala Harris]]'s numbers would rise.

(00:29:00) Real Clear Politics polling averages from June showed [[Joe Biden]] leading with 32%, followed by [[Bernie Sanders]] (17%), [[Elizabeth Warren]] (13%), [[Kamala Harris]] (7%), and [[Pete Buttigieg]] (7%).

(00:30:00) Analysis of post-debate momentum concluded that straight white male candidates like [[Joe Biden]], [[Bernie Sanders]], and [[Beto O'Rourke]] were declining, while candidates representing a "diversity coalition" like [[Kamala Harris]], [[Elizabeth Warren]], [[Pete Buttigieg]], and [[Cory Booker]] were ascendant.

(00:32:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] declared [[Kamala Harris]] the clear winner of the debate due to her calculated attack on [[Joe Biden]], with [[Pete Buttigieg]] as a secondary winner for a passable performance that boosted his name recognition.

(00:34:00) [[Andrew Yang]] was identified as a major loser for an unaggressive, monotone performance where he spoke only twice, failing to explain his signature Universal Basic Income policy effectively.

(00:35:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued the Democratic primary was now wide open, with the competitive field narrowing to [[Joe Biden]], [[Bernie Sanders]], [[Elizabeth Warren]], [[Kamala Harris]], [[Pete Buttigieg]], [[Cory Booker]], and [[Beto O'Rourke]].

(00:38:00) The analysis suggested [[Kamala Harris]] had moved into first or second place in terms of likelihood to win the nomination due to her debate performance, while [[Joe Biden]] was now fighting for his political life.
