## Summary

Representatives Jim Banks, Mark Meadows, Vicki Hartzler, and Brian Babin sent a letter to Attorney General Bill Barr urging the U.S. Department of Justice to prosecute hardcore internet pornography using obscenity laws. Nicholas J. Fuentes endorsed this congressional letter as a start to addressing pornography as a public health crisis. Fuentes also stated he would accept invitations to appear on podcasts hosted by Charlie Kirk or Ben Shapiro to expose his views to mainstream audiences.

---

(00:01:00) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] announced the featured story for the episode would be a discussion about banning pornography, a position his movement has held for years.

(00:01:45) [[Jim Banks]], [[Mark Meadows]], [[Vicki Hartzler]], and [[Brian Babin]] sent a letter to Attorney General [[Bill Barr]] urging the [[United States|U.S.]] Department of Justice to use obscenity laws to prosecute hardcore internet pornography.

(00:02:16) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] planned to discuss an appearance by [[Peter D'Abroska]] on [[Tucker Carlson]]'s show, where D'Abroska called out [[Turning Point USA]] and the [[Daily Wire]] and advocated for a 10-year moratorium on legal immigration.

(00:03:33) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] expressed concern that [[YouTube]]'s updated Terms of Service on December 10th could lead to a politically motivated purge of conservative channels, citing the recent banning of [[Owen Benjamin]] as potential evidence.

(00:07:51) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] directed his audience to his email list at NicholasJFuentes.com and his Telegram channel as the only guaranteed ways to follow him if he is banned from major platforms.

(00:08:50) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] traveled to [[New York City]] to appear in-studio on [[Milo Yiannopoulos|Milo]]'s show on [[Free Speech TV]], an interview he described as controversial for both of their audiences but ultimately fair and productive.

(00:10:40) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated he would accept invitations to appear on podcasts hosted by [[Charlie Kirk]] or [[Ben Shapiro]] to expose his views on demographic change, social conservatism, and the [[Israel]] lobby to mainstream audiences.

(00:14:23) [[Peter D'Abroska]], a former writer for [[Big League Politics]] and congressional candidate in [[North Carolina]]'s 7th district, appeared on [[Tucker Carlson]]'s Fox News program to advocate for a net-zero immigration moratorium.

(00:23:42) The letter from [[Jim Banks]], [[Mark Meadows]], [[Vicki Hartzler]], and [[Brian Babin]] cited [[Donald Trump]]'s campaign promise to enforce obscenity laws and noted that 15 state legislatures have declared pornography a public health crisis.

(00:24:17) The congressional letter argued that under Attorney General [[Eric Holder]], the [[United States|U.S.]] Department of Justice disbanded its obscenity prosecution task force, which had previously prioritized such cases under Attorney General [[Bill Barr]] in the early 1990s.

(00:41:50) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that libertarian conservatives who defend pornography on abstract First Amendment grounds are inconsistent, as they support government action on issues like foreign wars and anti-BDS laws but oppose it for tangible societal harms.

(00:44:43) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] endorsed the letter from [[Jim Banks]], [[Mark Meadows]], [[Vicki Hartzler]], and [[Brian Babin]] as a modest but important start to addressing the public health crisis of pornography, particularly its accessibility to children.

(01:03:10) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] noted that [[Matt Walsh]] of the [[Daily Wire]] also discussed banning pornography, but criticized him as an unreliable ally due to his past attacks on immigration critics and support for racial egalitarianism.
