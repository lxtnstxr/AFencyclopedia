## Summary

Donald Trump appointed Mick Mulvaney as White House Chief of Staff after considering Chris Christie, Nick Ayers, and Mark Meadows. The United States maintained approximately 2,000 combat troops in northeastern Syria, where Ambassador Nikki Haley stated U.S. policy was no longer focused on regime change. Nicholas J. Fuentes argued the Trump administration's post-midterm strategy failed to deliver on immigration promises and that the America First movement lacked necessary institutional resources.

(00:01:20) [[Donald Trump]] appointed [[Mick Mulvaney]] as his new White House Chief of Staff, replacing [[John Kelly]] who had resigned or been forced out the previous week.

(00:02:15) [[Donald Trump]] had also considered [[Chris Christie]], [[Nick Ayers]], and [[Mark Meadows]] for the Chief of Staff position before selecting [[Mick Mulvaney]].

(00:03:00) [[Mick Mulvaney]] was a former congressman and head of the [[Office of Management and Budget]] who had been a reliable ally of the [[Koch brothers]] while in Congress and was considered weak on immigration by his critics.

(00:03:40) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] stated that sources close to the president had informed him that [[Hope Hicks]], [[Mark Meadows]], or [[Bob Lighthizer]] would have been preferable Chief of Staff selections to [[Mick Mulvaney]].

(00:04:00) [[John Bolton]], appointed as National Security Advisor, had not exerted the expected neoconservative influence on [[Donald Trump]]'s foreign policy, as the [[United States]] had not invaded [[Syria]] or [[Iran]].

(00:08:40) The [[United States]] maintained approximately 2,000 combat troops and 5,000 contractors in northeastern [[Syria]] in a consensual arrangement with Kurdish forces, a presence critics argued constituted an invasion.

(00:09:40) [[Nikki Haley]], as [[United States]] Ambassador to the [[United Nations]], declared in April that American policy in [[Syria]] was no longer focused on regime change.

(00:12:40) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that [[Donald Trump]]'s post-midterm strategy of appeasing the establishment [[Republican Party]] and groups like the [[Heritage Foundation]] with tax cuts to fuel reelection had failed to deliver on core immigration promises like the border wall.

(00:13:20) The [[Donald Trump|Trump]] administration's push for $5 billion in border wall funding was less than a third of the White House's own estimated $17 billion total cost, a figure likely to double due to government inefficiency.

(00:14:10) Despite public criticism from commentators like [[Tucker Carlson]] and [[Ann Coulter]], the [[Donald Trump|Trump]] base's enthusiasm was waning due to disappointing personnel appointments and a lack of progress on key issues like immigration.

(00:16:30) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] identified a lack of institutional resources and organization as a chief problem for the America First movement, noting that necessary alignment between financiers, intellectuals, and political operatives had not yet occurred.

(00:17:30) Organizations like the [[National Policy Institute]] and [[American Renaissance]] possessed resources, but building a mainstream think tank required significant funding and a coordinated coalition currently fragmented between alt-right, alt-light, and establishment factions.

(00:32:40) [[Connor Lamb]], a Democrat who won a special election in [[Pennsylvania]]'s 18th congressional district, pitched himself as a moderate who would help [[Donald Trump]] but subsequently voted in line with the Democratic caucus against the president's agenda.

(00:32:50) Democratic Senator [[Heidi Heitkamp]] of [[North Dakota]] was defeated after voting against the confirmation of Supreme Court Justice [[Brett Kavanaugh]].

(00:33:20) Following the 2018 midterms, [[Republican Party|Republicans]] would need near-unanimous support from their caucus plus approximately 18 right-leaning Democrats to achieve a majority in the [[United States House of Representatives]], a scenario viewed as impossible.

(00:38:40) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] planned to attend the Conservative Political Action Conference (CPAC) and encouraged supporters who would form his entourage to present a clean-cut, "optical" appearance and avoid looking "rough."

(00:39:00) [[Richard Spencer]] had challenged [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] to a debate, a prospect Fuentes viewed as a confrontation rather than a legitimate debate, claiming the "optics war" within the right had been decisively won by his faction.

(00:39:20) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] argued that figures like [[Jared Taylor]] of [[American Renaissance]] and [[Faith Goldy]] who embraced "optics" were on strong trajectories, while those who opposed it had become disreputable.

(00:43:40) [[Jamal Khashoggi]] was a journalist and ally of the [[Muslim Brotherhood]] who had also associated with [[Osama bin Laden]] in the 1980s when bin Laden was part of the anti-Soviet Mujahideen.

(00:44:00) The [[Muslim Brotherhood]], which [[Jamal Khashoggi]] supported, was not a liberal organization, contradicting his portrayal as a liberal "reformer" in media coverage following his killing.

(00:47:40) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] suggested that as demographic changes made the [[United States]] resemble [[Brazil]], the resulting "banana republic" politics could open new possibilities for political strongmen or "Caesar" figures, representing a different kind of victory.

(01:07:40) [[Nicholas J. Fuentes]] defined the core distinction between right and left wing politics as order versus disorder, with the right embodying stability, tradition, and preservation of institutions, and the left embodying chaotic progress and revolution, a dichotomy originating in the [[French Revolution]].
